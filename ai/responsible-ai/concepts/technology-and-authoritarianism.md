# Technology and Authoritarianism — Do Artifacts Have Politics?

## The Starting Point

> "Nuclear technology could only ever be built alongside authoritarian social systems."

This claim — associated with Langdon Winner's 1980 essay _"Do Artifacts Have Politics?"_ —
is not about what a technology does. It is about what a technology _requires_.

The argument: some technologies embed social relations in their design. They are not
politically neutral. They can only be built, maintained, or scaled under specific kinds
of social organisation. The question is not "what does this technology enable?" but
"what conditions does this technology demand?"

---

## Winner's Argument — Nuclear Technology

Nuclear technology requires things that democratic, consensual processes cannot easily supply:

**Scale of resource compulsion.** Uranium enrichment, reactor construction, weapons
development, and waste management across centuries cannot be assembled by decentralised,
voluntary processes. The state must compel — land acquisition, labour, secrecy,
acceptance of risk by communities that did not choose it. The Manhattan Project
displaced Indigenous communities, irradiated Pacific Islanders during testing, and
classified its own health data from the workers it exposed. None of this happened
through consent.

**Structural secrecy.** Nuclear capability requires classified information and a permanent
security apparatus. A fully informed democratic debate about nuclear policy is impossible
because the information required to participate is classified. The technology structurally
excludes the public from decisions that affect them most.

**Transgenerational authority.** Nuclear waste remains dangerous for timescales that
dwarf any democratic institution. Managing it requires imposing decisions on people who
do not yet exist and cannot consent. Authority exercised without the possibility of
the governed having any say — that is the definition of authoritarianism in its
philosophical sense, regardless of the political system nominally in charge.

**Proliferation control as hierarchy.** Preventing nuclear weapons from spreading requires
surveillance, coercion, and the exercise of power by some states over others. The
non-proliferation regime is explicitly hierarchical — some states are permitted weapons,
others are not, enforced by threat. That hierarchy is not democratically negotiated.

## The Same Argument Applied to AI

Large-scale AI development requires things that democratic processes are slow and
messy at delivering:

**Mass data collection without meaningful consent.** The datasets required to train
frontier models cannot be assembled through fully consensual, compensated, democratically
governed processes. They were scraped, compelled, or extracted — from the open web,
from users who did not understand what they were agreeing to, from workers in the
Global South paid fractional wages to label content they found traumatising.

**Rapid infrastructure deployment.** Data centres, power grids, chip fabrication —
at the scale required for frontier AI — need land, water, and power at speeds that
override local opposition, environmental review, and community input. Democratic
processes with legal challenge rights are slow. The technology wants faster.

**Coordinated industrial policy.** State direction of capital, talent, and research
toward specific technological goals. Markets alone do not produce this — they produce
diffuse bets across many directions. Winning a technology race requires coordination
that markets underdeliver and authoritarian states overproduce.

**Long time horizons.** AI infrastructure investments won't pay off for decades.
Democratic electoral cycles of four to five years actively punish long-horizon investment
— politicians who spend on things that bear fruit after they leave office don't get
credit. Authoritarian systems with no electoral accountability can sustain longer bets.

**Suppression of honest feedback.** Keeping the narrative clean enough to maintain
public and investor confidence requires managing dissent — from researchers who
publish safety concerns, from workers who organise, from journalists who investigate.
The sycophancy problem encoded into institutions is structurally easier to maintain
when dissent has no protected channel.

## The China Case — Speed, Cost, and What's Hidden

China's technological speed in AI and urban development is real in specific domains.
Its structural source is also specific:

**No meaningful consent requirement for data collection.** The surveillance infrastructure
that makes Chinese AI so capable in computer vision, facial recognition, and behavioural
prediction was built on data collected from a population with no legal recourse to refuse.
The capability is real. The cost was borne by people who couldn't say no.

**State-directed capital.** The government designates semiconductors or AI as strategic
priorities and redirects industrial capacity accordingly — without waiting for market
signals, without shareholder approval, without public consultation. Speed follows.

**City development without property rights.** The cities that look impressive were built
by acquiring land at state-set prices with no right of appeal, relocating millions of
people on timescales impossible under legal challenge regimes. The output is visible.
The displaced people are not in the photographs.

**No liability for failure.** Empty city districts, abandoned infrastructure projects,
failed technology bets — in a system without democratic accountability, no one loses
power for these decisions. That removes the caution that accountability produces.
It also removes the correction mechanism that accountability provides.

> The impressive output is real. The hidden cost is also real.
> It is just borne by people who could not say no.

## The Democratic Dilemma

Western democracies face a genuine structural tension that is not hypothetical —
it is already producing policy decisions:

**Moving at democratic speed** means losing the AI race to systems that do not share
democratic values. Slower data collection, slower infrastructure, slower industrial
coordination.

**Moving at AI-race speed** means bypassing the democratic processes that distinguish
these systems from the ones they're competing with. The US CHIPS Act involved eminent
domain and fast-tracked environmental review. The EU AI Act took years of deliberation
and is already being lobbied into weakness. The gap between democratic deliberation
speed and technological deployment speed is a structural vulnerability.

**The incremental erosion risk.** If the technology requires conditions that democracy
finds hard to supply, competitive pressure from authoritarian AI creates internal pressure
on democracies to become more authoritarian in order to compete. Not through a dramatic
shift — through accumulated exceptions. Each emergency justifies a little more
centralisation, a little less oversight, a little more secrecy. The powers acquired
in each emergency are rarely fully returned.

This is exactly the mechanism that produced the post-9/11 surveillance state. The
emergency was real. The response exceeded what the emergency required. The character
of the system changed incrementally, through decisions that each seemed locally justified.

## Where Authoritarianism Has Hidden Costs

The speed advantage of authoritarian AI development is real in the early phase of a
technology race. In the long phase, specific structural problems accumulate:

**The sycophancy problem scales.** Systems that suppress honest feedback produce
brittle outputs — overfit to the conditions that produced them, unable to adapt when
those conditions change. Chinese semiconductor fabrication remains significantly behind
TSMC and Samsung at the leading edge, partly because the global supply chain and talent
mobility that advanced fabrication requires is inhibited by authoritarian systems.
The best researchers want to work in places where they can leave if they disagree.

**Honest error correction is slower.** Democratic systems with free press, academic
freedom, and legal challenge produce faster identification of what isn't working.
The feedback loop that corrects honest error requires channels that authoritarian
systems structurally close.

**The capability gap in specific domains.** Authoritarian speed produces impressive
results where the problem is well-defined and the objective function is clear —
infrastructure, surveillance, logistics. It underperforms where the problem requires
genuine creativity, interdisciplinary friction, and the willingness to publish findings
that contradict the state's preferred narrative.

> Authoritarianism optimises for speed of deployment at the expense of quality of consent.
> In the early phase of a technology race, speed looks like winning.
> In the long phase, the absence of honest feedback produces systems that are
> brittle, overfit, and unable to adapt.

## The Winner Question for AI

Winner's question was: do artifacts have politics? Are technologies politically neutral,
or do they embed and require specific social relations?

For AI, the question becomes:

> What social conditions does large-scale AI development require —
> and are those conditions compatible with the societies it claims to serve?

If the answer is that frontier AI requires mass non-consensual data extraction,
infrastructure deployment that overrides community consent, industrial coordination
that bypasses democratic deliberation, and suppression of honest safety feedback —
then the technology is not politically neutral. It requires conditions that are
in tension with democratic governance.

That does not mean democratic societies cannot build AI. It means they will build
it more slowly, with more friction, and with more genuine accountability for what
they build — which may ultimately produce better systems, at greater short-term cost.

Or it means the competitive pressure will cause them to suspend the democratic
conditions incrementally, until what they have built no longer resembles what
they started with.

Which of those happens is not a technical question.

---

## Key Insight

> The nuclear argument was: you cannot build this technology without the state
> compelling people who did not consent.
> The AI argument may be the same.
> The question is whether democracies will recognise this before the incremental
> exceptions have accumulated into a different kind of system.
