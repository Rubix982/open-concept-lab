# Rawls and Distributive Justice

*Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis. Not a reproduction of course material.*

---

## Who Is John Rawls

John Rawls — American political philosopher, Harvard University, 1921-2002.
Primary work: *A Theory of Justice* (1971). One of the most influential works
in political philosophy of the 20th century. Revived social contract theory
and gave it a rigorous modern framework.

His central question: **what principles should govern a just society?**

---

## The Veil of Ignorance — The Core Thought Experiment

Rawls asks: how would you design a just society if you did not know what
position you would occupy in it?

Imagine you are behind a **"veil of ignorance."** You know everything about
how societies work — institutions, economic systems, political arrangements —
but you do not know:

- What race you will be
- What gender
- What class you will be born into
- What natural talents you will have
- What your conception of the good life will be
- Which generation you will belong to

From behind this veil, you are asked to choose the principles that will
govern the society you are about to enter.

**Why this works as a thought experiment:**

Because you don't know your position, you cannot rationally design a system
that advantages your group. You might end up at the bottom. Rational
self-interest, applied behind the veil, produces principles designed to
protect the worst-off — because that might be you.

---

## The Two Principles Rawls Argues You Would Choose

### Principle 1 — Equal Basic Liberties

Everyone gets the same fundamental rights and freedoms:
- Freedom of speech and conscience
- Political participation rights
- Equal protection under the law

These are **lexically prior** — they cannot be traded off against economic
gains. A richer society that suppresses some people's political rights is
not more just. Basic liberties come first, always.

### Principle 2 — The Difference Principle

Social and economic inequalities are only just if they **benefit the least
advantaged members of society.**

This is the sharp one. It does not require equality of outcome. It permits
inequalities — but only when those inequalities make the worst-off better
off than they would be under a more equal arrangement.

**Simple example:**

Paying surgeons more than nurses attracts better people into surgery, producing
better healthcare for everyone including the poorest. That inequality — surgeon
vs. nurse pay — is just under Rawls, because it benefits the least advantaged.

But paying CEOs 400 times what their workers earn, when that gap does not
produce better outcomes for the worst-off, is unjust under Rawls — even if
nobody is actively harmed. The inequality must justify itself by lifting the floor.

---

## The Maximin Rule

Rawls's decision rule behind the veil: **maximise the minimum.**

Design the society so that the worst possible position is as good as it can be.

Not the average. Not the total aggregate welfare. The floor.

This is why the Rawlsian position is described as producing a "sufficient"
floor rather than equal distribution. The worst-off position should be as
high as possible. Above that floor, inequalities are permitted if they serve
the Difference Principle.

## Distribution — The Allocative Framework

Rawls provides a rigorous framework for thinking about distributive justice —
who should get what, and on what grounds.

**"Goods"** in this framework include everything from the concrete to the abstract:
- Air, food, shelter — material necessities
- Opportunities, education, healthcare — enabling goods
- Political rights, social recognition — participatory goods

**Maldistribution** — distributing goods on morally irrelevant grounds —
is the core injustice. When someone is denied medical care because of race,
or prosecuted for tax fraud because of citizenship status, or excluded from
employment because of characteristics irrelevant to the job — that is
maldistribution, and it causes harm.

**The distributive harm argument in formal terms:**

1. If someone doesn't get something they should have gotten for morally
   irrelevant reasons, they've been treated unfairly
2. If someone is treated unfairly, they've been harmed
3. Therefore, maldistribution on morally irrelevant grounds causes harm

---

## A Two-Part Distribution Framework — Beyond Rawls

Rawls provides the floor and the equality of liberties. A more complete
framework adds a second dimension:

**Part 1 — The floor (Rawlsian):**
Everyone gets sufficient access to the goods that make a decent life possible.
Non-negotiable. Below this floor, nobody falls — regardless of contribution,
conduct, or characteristics.

**Part 2 — Reduced distribution to harm-causers:**
Goods flowing to actors who use them to harm others enable more harm.
Resources given to institutions that exploit children enable more exploitation.
Capital flowing to polluters enables more pollution.

The distribution is not neutral — it is either enabling or constraining harm.
This is the polluter pays principle as a distribution principle: those causing
harm should receive less of the common resource because they are already taking
more than their share by externalising costs.

Together: a floor nobody falls below, and a ceiling on harm-enablement.
Between them: distribution can reflect contribution, need, and desert
depending on the domain and the goods in question.

## The Rizq Connection — Personal Accountability as Distribution

The Islamic concept of rizq — divinely ordained provision — adds a layer
that Rawls's structural framework does not contain: **personal accountability
for what flows through you.**

Rawls designs principles for institutions. The rizq framing makes every
individual a node in the distribution network who is personally accountable
for what they withhold or enable.

An employer who denies someone employment on morally irrelevant grounds —
who they love, what they look like, what they believe — is not just violating
an institutional principle. They will be asked: why did you prevent this
person's rizq?

The algorithm that does the same thing at scale is producing the same harm,
invisibly, across millions of decisions — and cannot be asked. That is why
the algorithm is insufficient as a governance mechanism. It distributes
without accountability. It causes distributive harm without a responsible
actor who can be held to account.

## How Rawls Applies to AI Distribution Systems

Algorithmic systems that distribute goods are distribution mechanisms in the
Rawlsian sense:

- **Credit scoring** — distributes access to capital
- **Hiring algorithms** — distributes employment and opportunity
- **Healthcare resource allocation** — distributes medical care
- **Criminal justice risk assessment** — distributes liberty and incarceration
- **Content recommendation** — distributes attention, information, and social connection

When these systems distribute on morally irrelevant grounds — race, zip code,
name, school prestige, proxy signals for protected characteristics — they
produce distributive harm in Rawls's exact definition.

**The veil of ignorance test for algorithmic systems:**

Would you design this algorithm if you did not know which side of its decisions
you would end up on? If you might be the person denied the loan, the job,
the bail reduction, the medical referral?

Most current algorithmic distribution systems fail this test — because they
were designed by people who knew they would not be on the receiving end of
the algorithm's adverse decisions.

## The Rawls Critique — Where the Framework Has Limits

**The neutral baseline problem:**

Rawls designed his framework for stable, well-ordered societies with functioning
institutions. Algorithmic systems operate in societies where institutions are
already structured by historical injustice. The baseline from which the veil
of ignorance operates is not neutral.

If you don't know your position but you know the society has a history of
racial discrimination in lending — would you design a colour-blind algorithm?
Or one that corrects for the accumulated effects of that discrimination?

A colour-blind algorithm applied to a society with colour-coded history
produces colour-coded outcomes while claiming neutrality. Rawls's framework
does not fully resolve whether the correction for historical injustice should
be built into the algorithm or handled elsewhere in the institutional structure.
This is what his students and critics have argued about for fifty years.

**The individual vs. structural tension:**

Rawls is primarily concerned with institutional structure — what rules govern
distribution. He is less concerned with individual conduct within those
institutions. His framework tells you what a just institution looks like.
It is less precise about what a just individual does when operating within
an unjust institution.

The rizq framing fills this gap: even within unjust structures, individuals
retain personal accountability for what they withhold or enable.

**The scope problem for AI:**

Rawls imagined rational actors choosing principles for a society they will
enter. AI systems distribute goods without rational agency, without the
capacity to reason about justice, and without accountability for the outcomes
they produce. The Rawlsian framework requires agents who can be held to the
principles they chose. Algorithms cannot be held to anything.

This is the fundamental governance gap: the distribution mechanism has been
separated from the accountability mechanism. Rawls's framework assumes they
are in the same entity. In algorithmic distribution, they are not.

---

## Key Insight

> Rawls asks: what would you choose if you didn't know your position?
> Most algorithmic distribution systems were designed by people who knew
> they would never be on the adverse side of the decisions they were designing.
>
> The veil of ignorance is not just a philosophical tool.
> It is the test every algorithm should face before deployment:
> would the people building it accept its decisions if they didn't know
> which side they would be on?
>
> Almost none of them would.
> That is the distributive justice problem in AI, stated simply.
