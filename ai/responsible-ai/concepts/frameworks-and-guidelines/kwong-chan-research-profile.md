## Who He Is

**Kwong Chan** — Academic Specialist in Marketing at the D'Amore-McKim School of
Business, Northeastern University, and Executive Director of the **DMSB AI Strategic
Hub (DASH)** — the school's initiative for embedding AI across curriculum, research,
and corporate outreach. Formerly Associate Director in the Telecommunication and
Technology Industry Practice Group at Nielsen, and marketing professor at UMass Amherst.

**PhD:** Michigan State University

**Industry background:** Nielsen analytics → academic. A practitioner-to-scholar
career path, which shapes his approach: AI for strategic decision making is not
primarily a theoretical question for him — it is a question he has answered in
applied contexts for Samsung, VISA, Nike, Goodyear, ASUS, ACER, and General Motors.

## The DATA Initiative / DASH

**DMSB AI Strategic Hub (DASH)** is D'Amore-McKim's school-wide strategic programme
for embedding AI across all operations.

**Mission:** "Ensure intelligent technologies permeate D'Amore-McKim's curriculum,
research efforts, corporate outreach, and contributions to the larger society."

**Three operating assumptions underlying DASH:**
1. AI creates measurable business value
2. AI augments human capabilities holistically rather than replacing them
3. AI facilitates a shift from content generation to genuine knowledge creation

**Four strategic pillars:**

| Pillar | Focus |
|---|---|
| AI for Research | Advancing scholarship via intelligent technologies |
| AI for Teaching | Transforming educational delivery and assessment |
| AI for Business | Preparing students for AI-driven workplaces |
| AI for Good | Applying AI to societal challenges |

**Active projects:**
- **DASH_Box** — computational tool for data analysis and research
- **Essaybot** — AI-assisted grading platform, piloted by faculty
- **CatBot** — student project for animal shelter adoption outreach
- **Prison Project** — AI-assisted programme helping incarcerated individuals develop job skills

The Prison Project and CatBot are worth noting: they represent the "AI for Good"
pillar applied to populations almost never centred in business school AI initiatives.
This suggests Chan's DASH has genuine scope beyond enterprise productivity.

**What the journal mix reveals:**
The breadth is unusual. IEEE TEM is engineering-facing and quantitative — getting
published there from a marketing background requires strong technical/analytical
credibility. JPIM is the premier innovation management outlet. JIM and JSM suggest
applied work in specific industry contexts. JPPM and JAd suggest interest in the
social and policy dimensions of marketing, not just the commercial ones.

This is not a researcher who stays in one lane. The combination of engineering
management, innovation, international marketing, and public policy suggests someone
who asks how analytics and innovation interact with broader systems — markets,
institutions, international contexts, public welfare.

## AI for Strategic Decision Making — What His Background Suggests

No specific published talks or articles on AI strategy were retrievable. But his
role and background suggest a specific perspective:

**From DASH's operating philosophy:** AI augments human capabilities rather than
replacing them. AI facilitates genuine knowledge creation rather than content
generation. These are not neutral statements — they are a position on the
understanding problem (topic 11), the human-centered AI argument (Shneiderman),
and the Mentat/Bene Gesserit framing we discussed.

**From his Nielsen background:** The Telecom and Technology Industry Practice Group
means he spent time doing real analytics work on competitive market dynamics,
consumer behaviour, and strategic positioning for technology companies — before
the current AI wave made "AI for business" a curriculum buzzword.

**The implication for module 6:** His approach to AI for strategic decision making
will likely be grounded, applied, and sceptical of hype — a practitioner who knows
what analytics can and cannot do in real industry contexts, building on research
that spans innovation, international markets, and policy. Not "AI will transform
everything" but "here is what the data actually shows, and here is how to make a
better decision with it."

## Connection to Course Themes

**Module 6 — AI for Strategic Decision Making:**
Chan brings the practitioner lens that has been largely absent from the other module
contributors. Where Canca comes from bioethics, Lapedriza from computer vision, and
Ricks from philosophy — Chan comes from Nielsen analytics and industry consulting.
His perspective on AI for strategic decisions is grounded in what actually works
when the client is Samsung and the question is "what should we build next?"

**The tension worth watching:** DASH's "AI augments, not replaces" framing is the
HCAI position we have documented throughout the course. But it is also the framing
that the FANG hiring argument, the worker surveillance argument, and the Amazon
business strategy analysis all complicate. Does augmentation produce the top-right
quadrant (Shneiderman) or does it produce the Labour monitoring / algorithmic
management pattern? Chan's industry background means he has seen both.

**The Prison Project connection:** This is the most interesting data point. An
AI initiative led by someone with a business analytics background that includes
a programme for incarcerated individuals is making a specific statement about
whose problems are worth solving with AI capability. That is the Venn diagram
principle applied to DASH itself — and it connects to the excluded perspectives
argument we built in module 4.

## Key Insight

> Chan sits at the intersection of three things that rarely coexist:
> deep quantitative analytics credibility (IEEE TEM, Nielsen),
> genuine innovation research (JPIM, new product development),
> and institutional leadership on AI education (DASH Executive Director).
>
> His perspective on AI for strategic decision making is likely the most
> practically grounded in the course — not because the other contributors
> are impractical, but because his background is explicitly about what
> analytics produces when the client has a real business decision to make
> and needs an answer that holds up under scrutiny.
>
> The question module 6 is probably asking: given everything AI can do,
> how do you use it to make better strategic decisions — not faster ones,
> not cheaper ones, but better ones?
> That is a harder question than it sounds, and Chan is the right person to ask it.
