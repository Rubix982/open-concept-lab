# Ethics as Defanging — How "AI Ethics" Neutralises Accountability

_Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis. Not a reproduction of course material._

---

## The Core Observation

By framing AI accountability as "ethics" — abstract, philosophical, contested, unprovable
— a specific political outcome is achieved before any specific argument is made.

Ethics is what philosophers debate around a fire. Policy is what governments enforce.
The moment you call AI accountability "ethics," you have shifted it from the domain of
law, rights, and enforcement into the domain of opinion and preference.

Nobody gets fined for having the wrong ethics.
Nobody goes to jail for an ethics violation.
The framing itself defangs the critique before it can become regulation.

---

## What the "Ethics" Frame Achieves

**1. Infinite deferral.**
Ethical questions are never fully settled — that is their nature. So "we are working
on the ethics" becomes a permanent holding position. There is always another framework
to develop, another principle to refine, another workshop to convene, another advisory
board to constitute. Meanwhile the product ships, the data is collected, and the harm
accumulates. The ethical discussion is not a path to accountability. It is a substitute
for it.

**2. Defanging through abstraction.**
Real impacts — job displacement, algorithmic discrimination, surveillance, manipulation
of political opinion — get translated into philosophical puzzles. The person whose job
was automated does not have an ethics problem. They have an economic problem, a rights
problem, a policy problem. Translating it into ethics makes it sound like a seminar
topic rather than a harm requiring remedy.

**3. Cover for the people causing harm.**
"We have an ethics team" has become a credible response to criticism of harmful AI
systems. An ethics team with no power to stop a product launch, no enforcement
authority, and no independence from the business is not accountability. It is the
_appearance_ of accountability, produced specifically to prevent actual accountability.
The team exists to absorb criticism, not to act on it.

**4. Attracting and managing serious people.**
Philosophers and ethicists who want to engage seriously get jobs inside companies,
get funding, get conference slots — and in exchange produce work sophisticated enough
to seem credible but toothless enough never to threaten the business model. The ones
whose work actually threatens the model lose access and funding. The selection pressure
is not toward honesty. It is toward sophisticated harmlessness.

**5. Eliminating the option of consequences.**
If AI accountability is ethics, the alternative to bad ethics is not enforcement —
it is better ethics. The frame removes consequences from the available vocabulary.
You cannot fine someone for failing an ethics framework. You cannot prosecute a
company for violating a principle. The frame ensures that even when problems are
identified, the response stays inside the domain of discussion rather than moving
into the domain of enforcement.

## The Think Tank Parallel

The "ethics" frame operates the same way the "government overreach" frame operates
in regulatory debates — and both are produced by the same institutional machinery.

**How think tanks work:**

1. A corporation or billionaire has a policy problem — a regulation to kill, a market
   to open, a tax to reduce
2. They fund an institution with an academic-sounding name
3. The institution produces "research" reaching the predetermined conclusion
4. The research gets cited in Congressional testimony, op-eds, and policy documents
   as independent expert opinion
5. The actual funder appears in a footnote nobody reads

The "independent research" is the product. The academic aesthetic is the packaging.
The policy outcome was the order placed before the research began.

**The Overton Window function.**
Think tanks don't just win arguments — they shift what counts as a legitimate position.
If enough well-funded institutions publish papers saying "government regulation of tech
is harmful," that position starts to seem reasonable, centrist, regardless of its merits.
The goal is not to win a single debate. It is to move the centre of the debate until
the starting position — "corporations should operate without meaningful oversight" —
sounds like common sense rather than a specific interest.

**The distraction function.**
While the public debate is about abstract principles — freedom, innovation, overreach,
ethics — the specific regulation that triggered the funding campaign either dies quietly
or gets watered down in committee. The philosophical debate is the smoke. The lobbying
is the fire. Ethics discourse functions the same way: while the debate is about
frameworks and principles, the product ships and the harm accumulates.

**Why it works even when belief is genuine.**
The manufactured argument is effective partly because many people at every level
genuinely believe it. A think tank paper gets cited by a senator who genuinely thinks
deregulation is good, who was educated by economists who genuinely believed the models,
who got grants from foundations that genuinely share the ideology. The belief is real
at every level. The funding just ensures certain beliefs get amplified and others do not.

## The Regulatory Debate — Why "Overreach" Only Appears in Tech

The "government overreach" argument in regulation is not a universal political principle.
It is a geographically and historically specific product:

**Where it dominates:** United States — specifically where deregulation benefits large
corporate donors. UK post-Thatcher to a lesser extent.

**Where it barely exists:**

- Japan — government actively shapes industry, infrastructure, urban development
- Germany — strong regulatory state, strong economy, no contradiction seen
- South Korea — state-directed industrial policy built its entire tech sector
- Nordic countries — high regulation, high trust, high quality of life
- Singapore — one of the most regulated cities on earth, held as a model of efficiency
- Most of the Global South — the argument is usually "we need _more_ government capacity"

The argument does not appear in food safety debates, aviation safety debates, building
code debates, pharmaceutical testing debates, or city planning debates — anywhere in
the world. It appears specifically and selectively when regulation threatens a profitable
industry with the resources to fund the argument.

**The internal contradiction.**
The same political culture that argues government shouldn't regulate tech has no problem
with government subsidising semiconductor manufacturing, protecting pharmaceutical
patents, bailing out banks, funding the internet through DARPA, or contracting AI
development through defence agencies. Government intervention is fine when it flows
toward corporations. It becomes "overreach" when it flows toward protecting citizens
from those same corporations.

**The tax contract.**
Citizens fund the state. The state uses those resources to protect citizens from harm —
including harm from powerful actors. A government that collects taxes but refuses to
legislate in the public interest on the grounds that legislation "expands government
power" has broken the contract. It is taking the revenue and withholding the protection.

Infrastructure, zoning, food safety, building codes, environmental protection — globally,
these are understood as the basic work of government. Tokyo's skyscrapers stand beside
decades-old noodle shops because the government decided both had a right to exist and
built the legal framework to protect that coexistence. No one calls it tyranny.

The idea that tech should be exempt from the same logic is not a principled position.
It is an interest position, held by people who have successfully framed their interest
as a principle.

## What Honest AI Accountability Would Look Like

The honest vocabulary for AI accountability is not ethics. It is:

| Term               | What it means                                                      | Why it matters                               |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| **Rights**         | Things people are entitled to regardless of what a company prefers | Not negotiable, not subject to ethics review |
| **Law**            | Enforceable rules with consequences for violation                  | Produces actual deterrence                   |
| **Liability**      | The people who caused harm bear the cost of it                     | Aligns incentives correctly                  |
| **Representation** | The people affected have power in decisions that affect them       | Fixes the excluded perspectives problem      |

None of these words appear as frequently as "ethics" in AI governance discourse.
That is not an accident. Each of them implies consequences. "Ethics" does not.

## Canca's Position — Working From Inside

Cansu Canca's PiE model is a genuine attempt to fight this from the inside — to make
ethics operational, concrete, and actionable rather than abstract and deferrable.
The core/instrumental distinction, The Box, the four-service model — these are attempts
to give ethics teeth it doesn't normally have.

But even well-designed ethics tools operate within a context where the companies
deploying them have already decided what outcomes they can tolerate. The tool can be
honest. The context can still neutralise it. A company that hires an ethics consultant,
considers their recommendations, and then proceeds anyway has technically "done ethics."
The consultant produced a process. The process produced no change. The appearance of
accountability was generated. The accountability was not.

The PiE model's limitation is not in its design. It is in who controls the decision
to implement it, and who bears the cost when it is ignored.

## Structural Interventions — What Would Actually Work

There is no single solution. Anyone offering one is selling something. But there are
interventions that are structurally sound — not because they fix everything, but because
they attack the right problems at the right level.

### At the Legal and Enforcement Level

Stop treating AI harms as ethics problems and start treating them as liability problems.
When a hiring algorithm discriminates, the company that deployed it should be liable for
the discrimination — same standard as a human manager. When a recommendation system
causes documented psychological harm, the platform bears the cost.

Liability changes incentives faster than any principles document. It is why pharmaceutical
companies run safety trials — not because they are ethical, but because the alternative
is expensive. The same logic applies here.

### At the Governance Design Level

- Mandatory representation of affected communities in regulatory processes — with actual
  decision-making power, not advisory roles
- Funded by the state, not the industry — so the funding source does not determine the outcome
- Cooling-off periods on the revolving door between industry and regulatory bodies
- Independent technical review bodies with genuine independence from those they review

### At the Data Level

Minimum necessary data as an absolute for harm-recognised product categories — not a
guideline, an absolute with criminal enforcement. Age verification without identity.
No opt-in pathway for broader collection. The pharmaceutical model applied to data:
justify what you need before you collect anything, not after.
_(See: concepts/categorical-data-exclusion.md for the full argument.)_

### At the Market Level

Break up the compute concentration. The AI capabilities race runs on infrastructure
controlled by three or four companies. That concentration is itself a governance problem
— not because those companies are uniquely evil, but because no accountability system
works when the regulated entity is larger than the regulating body. Antitrust is a
blunt tool but it is the right category of intervention.

### At the Educational Level

The intervention that takes longest and matters most. People who can read a principles
document and ask "whose values, decided by whom, enforced how, at whose cost" are the
immune system of any governance system. You cannot manufacture them. You can only create
conditions where they develop — which is what serious education in this field should do,
and what most responsible AI discourse currently fails to do.

### The Taleb Standard — Skin in the Game

Nassim Nicholas Taleb's core principle from _Skin in the Game_ (2018): you should not
be able to impose risk on others while bearing none yourself.

- The economist who prescribes austerity for a country they'll never live in
- The general who sends soldiers to wars they won't fight
- The AI governance class that deploys systems they'll never be subject to

If you have no downside when you're wrong, your recommendations cannot be trusted —
not because you're dishonest, but because the incentive to be careful simply doesn't
exist. Apply this standard when evaluating who to trust in any governance conversation:
does this person bear any cost if they are wrong?

**Who Taleb is:** Lebanese Greek Orthodox Christian intellectual — Nassim (Arabic:
"gentle breeze"), Nicholas (Greek, common among Levantine Christians), Taleb (Arabic:
"seeker of knowledge" — same root as Taliban, which simply means "students"). Formed
by Arabic culture, Mediterranean Christianity, French colonial education, then Wharton.
Former options trader — his ideas about risk come from real money, real consequences,
real skin in the game. His family were traders and politicians in northern Lebanon.
He watched Lebanon tear itself apart while its political class bore none of the cost.
That biographical detail is not incidental to his ideas. It is their source.

Key books in order of relevance to this course:

- _Antifragile_ (2012) — systems that gain from disorder; humans are antifragile;
  engineering comfort into society engineers fragility into people
- _Skin in the Game_ (2018) — accountability requires consequence; trust requires risk
- _The Black Swan_ (2007) — rare unpredictable events with massive consequences that
  are systematically underestimated before and overexplained after

### What Will Not Work

- Principles documents without enforcement
- Ethics teams without veto power
- Voluntary commitments from companies with financial incentives to ignore them
- Waiting for the industry to self-correct
- Hoping that the people currently in the room will voluntarily expand who is in the room

### The Honest Summary

The problem is a power problem dressed as a technical problem dressed as an ethics
problem. Power problems are solved by redistributing power — through law, liability,
representation, and breaking up concentrations. Everything else is useful but secondary.

The ethics conversation is worth having. It just cannot be the only conversation,
or the final one.

---

## Key Insight

> "Ethical AI" achieves something precise: it keeps the conversation in the domain
> of philosophy, where consequences do not exist, and out of the domain of law,
> where they do.
>
> The people who benefit most from this framing are not philosophers.
> They are the companies whose products would be regulated if the conversation
> moved from "what are our values?" to "what are the enforceable rules?"
>
> Ethics is necessary. It is not sufficient. And when it is offered as a substitute
> for rights, law, liability, and representation — it is being used as a shield,
> not a compass.
