# Employer Monitoring and Workplace Surveillance

*Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis.*

---

## The Surface Argument

The employer pays for the employee's time. Monitoring verifies that time is being
spent on work. This seems analogous to any other quality assurance process: you
check that what you paid for was delivered.

The argument sounds reasonable on the surface. It collapses on examination.

---

## Five Points Where the Argument Fails

### 1. Monitoring Encodes a Prior Judgment of Guilt

A manager who installs real-time monitoring on an employee's computer before
observing any performance problem has already decided the employee cannot be
trusted. The monitoring is not neutral verification — it is surveillance premised
on suspicion.

A relationship premised on suspicion is not an employment relationship. It is
supervision of a person assumed to be dishonest until proven otherwise.

Trust is not just a pleasant feature of a working relationship. It is the condition
under which meaningful work is possible. A person who knows every keystroke is
counted, every idle minute flagged, every application opened logged, cannot do the
kind of thinking that produces good work. They can only do the kind of work that
*looks* good under surveillance — which is a completely different thing.

### 2. The Argument Confuses Outputs with Activities

What the employer actually purchased is the employee's capability applied to specific
deliverables — code written, analysis completed, clients served, problems solved.
They did not purchase the right to observe every moment of the cognitive process
that produces those outputs.

A novelist on a deadline spends time staring at the ceiling.
A software engineer solves a hard problem in the shower.
A consultant's best thinking happens on a walk.

Activity monitoring that flags "idle time" is measuring the wrong thing entirely.
The employer who is measuring it has confused the input (time at desk, keystrokes
per hour) with the output (problem solved, deliverable completed). These are not
the same. Optimising for the measurable input destroys the conditions for the
valuable output.

### 3. The Contextual Integrity Violation

Using Nissenbaum's framework: the employment context has specific norms.
The employee discloses their professional work to the employer. The transmission
principle is **professional accountability** — you can see my outputs, you can
evaluate my performance, you can hold me to delivery standards.

The norm does not include continuous behavioural surveillance covering every
micro-activity throughout the working day. That transmission principle belongs
to a different context — the context of monitoring a subject of suspicion,
not the context of an employment relationship between consenting adults with
mutual obligations.

Installing continuous activity monitoring is a unilateral rewrite of the
transmission principle, imposed without renegotiation of the relationship.
The employee agreed to professional accountability. They did not agree to
behavioural surveillance.

### 4. The Power Asymmetry the Argument Ignores

The employer already holds multiple forms of power the employee does not:
- Power to terminate
- Power to assign tasks and set conditions
- Power to determine pay and benefits
- Power to promote or deny promotion
- Power to give references that shape the employee's future options

The monitoring claim extends that power into **total informational asymmetry**.
The employer now knows everything about the employee's moment-to-moment behaviour.
The employee knows very little about the employer's decision-making, pay scales,
financial health, or plans.

This is not mutual accountability. It is one-directional control.

Taleb's skin in the game applies: the employer is not being monitored in return.
The manager who monitors employees is not themselves monitored by the employees
they supervise. The asymmetry is structural, not incidental.

### 5. What Monitoring Actually Produces

The empirical evidence on workplace monitoring is fairly consistent:

- Increases stress and anxiety
- Reduces intrinsic motivation — people work for the metric, not the goal
- Increases presenteeism — appearing to work rather than working
- Reduces creativity and discretionary effort
- Increases turnover, especially among high performers
- Produces gaming of the metrics rather than improvement of actual performance

The people most harmed by monitoring are often the best employees — the ones
whose work requires autonomy, judgment, and periods of apparent inactivity that
produce genuine insight. The people least harmed are those doing the most mechanical,
easily measurable tasks — who were never the ones the employer was worried about.

Monitoring selects for compliance. Compliance and quality are different things.
Surveillance optimises for the former while destroying the conditions for the latter.

## The Trust and Goodwill Problem

The manager who installs monitoring has communicated something that cannot be
unsaid: *I do not believe you will do your job unless I watch you.*

The employee now knows they are regarded as untrustworthy unless proven otherwise.
The goodwill that motivates discretionary effort — going beyond the minimum,
solving problems the job description did not anticipate, caring about the outcome
rather than just the task — is damaged or gone.

This is not sentimentality. It is a practical observation about what produces good
work. The most valuable things an employee does are often the things that cannot
be easily measured: the insight that prevents a bad decision, the question that
reframes a problem, the relationship that brings in a client, the early warning
that something is wrong.

These require the employee to care — about the outcome, the organisation, the
mission. Monitoring communicates that the employer does not trust the employee.
The employee's caring response to that communication is entirely rational:
if I am not trusted, I will perform compliance and stop caring about outcomes.

## The Contextual Integrity Analysis in Full

**Context:** Employment relationship between professional adults.

**What the context's norms include:**
- Employer has access to outputs — deliverables, work product, performance evaluations
- Employer can set expectations about quality, timeliness, and standards
- Employer can terminate the relationship when outputs are consistently inadequate
- Employee has access to what is required of them and the resources to do it

**What the context's norms do not include:**
- Continuous monitoring of moment-to-moment behaviour
- Logging of every application opened, every website visited, every keystroke
- Real-time flags for "idle time" or deviations from expected activity patterns
- Surveillance of personal activities conducted on personal devices during break time

**The transmission principle that real-time monitoring asserts:**
- Every behaviour during working hours is the employer's legitimate concern
- The employee's attention, focus, and digital activity are owned by the employer
  during the hours purchased

**Why this transmission principle is wrong:**
- It was not part of the agreed relationship at hiring
- It applies to cognitive work a framework designed for assembly line work
  (where the relationship between time-on-task and output is direct and measurable)
- It produces the wrong outcomes even on its own terms (compliance not quality)
- It extends employer power into domains the employment relationship does not reach

## The Legitimate Version of Employer Interest

An employer has a legitimate interest in knowing whether the work is being done well.

That interest is fully served by **output evaluation**:
- Are deliverables arriving on time?
- Are they at appropriate quality?
- Is the employee responsive to feedback?
- Are they meeting the standards the role requires?

This is professional accountability. It is entirely legitimate and has been the
basis of employment relationships since professional work existed.

An employer does **not** have a legitimate interest in:
- Whether the employee spent seven minutes reading news at 10am
- Whether they opened a personal email
- How many keystrokes they made in an hour
- What applications they had open at 2pm on a Tuesday

Activity monitoring dressed as output accountability is a power claim. The dressing
conceals what is underneath: the employer's desire to extend control beyond the
legitimate scope of the employment relationship into the employee's cognitive process
and moment-to-moment attention.

## The AI Dimension — Algorithmic Management

The monitoring concern is now inseparable from the algorithmic management concern
documented in the occupational safety notes.

**Amazon warehouses:** The system generates automatic termination warnings based on
productivity metrics without requiring supervisor review. Workers are not monitored
by a human manager who can exercise judgment — they are monitored by an algorithm
that cannot. The transmission principle has shifted from professional accountability
(supervisor evaluates work) to algorithmic surveillance (system flags deviations
from metrics).

**Gig economy:** Platforms like Uber and DoorDash set rates, assign work, and
penalise drivers algorithmically — with no human supervisor who can be asked
why, no appeal mechanism that reaches a decision-maker with authority to revise,
and no visibility into the rules governing the system.

**"Bossware" in knowledge work:** Tools like Teramind, Hubstaff, and ActivTrak
log keystrokes, capture periodic screenshots, record application usage, and flag
"unproductive" time — creating a continuous surveillance layer over knowledge
workers whose outputs are complex, delayed, and difficult to reduce to activity metrics.

The AI layer makes the monitoring problem worse in two ways:

1. **Scale and granularity:** AI-powered monitoring can process vastly more data
   than human supervisors — producing a surveillance architecture that human
   management never could, even if it wanted to

2. **Accountability disappears:** A human supervisor who makes a bad call can be
   challenged, can explain their reasoning, and can revise their judgment. An
   algorithm that flags your productivity as insufficient offers none of these.
   The decision is presented as objective, the reasoning is opaque, and the
   appeal path is unclear or nonexistent.

## The EU AI Act Response

The EU AI Act classifies AI used for emotion recognition in workplaces as an
**unacceptable risk** — prohibited as of February 2025.

AI used for recruiting, allocating work, evaluating performance, or deciding on
promotion is classified as **high risk** — requiring mandatory risk management,
transparency requirements, human oversight, and prohibition on discrimination.

Workers must be informed about what data is collected, how it is processed, how
human oversight is ensured, and how automated decisions are made. They have the
right to request explanation and contest decisions.

The EU Platform Work Directive (December 2024) goes further for gig workers:
prohibits dismissal based solely on an algorithmic decision — a human must be
involved. Prohibits processing emotional or psychological data via algorithms.

These are the structural responses that the monitoring debate requires: not
guidelines and best practices, but binding prohibitions on the most invasive
forms combined with mandatory transparency and human oversight for the rest.

## Key Insight

> The employer monitoring argument sounds like quality assurance.
> It is actually a power claim — the assertion that the employer's purchase
> of professional time extends into ownership of the employee's moment-to-moment
> cognitive process and attention.
>
> This claim is wrong on its own terms: monitoring produces compliance, not quality.
> It is wrong contextually: the employment relationship's norms do not include
> continuous behavioural surveillance.
> It is wrong structurally: it extends already asymmetric power further without
> providing the employee any reciprocal accountability over the employer.
>
> The trust that makes good work possible cannot coexist with surveillance
> that assumes dishonesty. Once the monitoring is installed, the relationship
> has changed. The goodwill that produced discretionary effort is gone.
> What remains is performance for the camera — which is not the thing
> the employer needed in the first place.
