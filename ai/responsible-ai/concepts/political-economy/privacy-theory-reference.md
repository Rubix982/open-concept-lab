## The Dimensions of Privacy (Taxonomy)

Before the scholars, a structural map of what privacy is:

| Dimension | What it protects | Primary threat |
|---|---|---|
| **Aggregation** | Against assembling combined information profiles | Data brokers, surveillance systems |
| **Collection** | Against acquiring information in the first place | Sensors, scraping, harvesting |
| **Confidentiality** | Against disclosure of information already shared | Leaks, re-identification, data sales |
| **Control** | Against others accessing information without authorisation | Hacks, unauthorised access |
| **Solitude** | Against intrusion into physical or mental space | Ambient monitoring, constant availability |
| **Veillance** | Against being watched/observed | Surveillance, dataveillance, facial recognition |

**The veillance family (Steve Mann, 2013):**
- **Surveillance** — watching from above; powerful watching the less powerful (state watching citizens, employer watching workers)
- **Sousveillance** — watching from below; individuals watching powerful institutions (citizens recording police)
- **Dataveillance** (Roger Clarke, 1988) — monitoring through data trails without cameras (credit records, location data, browsing history)
- **Uberveillance** (M.G. and Katina Michael) — embedded, pervasive, always-on monitoring layer

The surveillance inversion we identified: democratic accountability requires sousveillance — citizens watching powerful institutions. What surveillance technology has produced is the opposite: maximum dataveillance of ordinary citizens, maximum opacity for the surveilling institutions.

## James Rachels — "Why Privacy Is Important" (1975)

*Philosophy & Public Affairs, Vol. 4, No. 4. One of the founding texts of privacy philosophy.*

### The Relationships Argument

Rachels rejects the purely instrumental view — that privacy matters only to prevent embarrassment or harm. His core claim: **privacy is constitutive of social relationships, not merely protective of interests**.

Different relationships are constituted by different patterns of disclosure. There is no single uniform self presented to everyone. Who you are in relation to another person is partly defined by what you share and what you withhold:

- A man is playful with his children, businesslike with employees, respectful with his mother-in-law, and shows a side to close friends that others never see
- A husband and wife behave differently when observed than when alone — not because they are hiding something wrong, but because the relationship is partly constituted by that differential access
- An employer has a legitimate interest in knowing an employee's salary — but this is a bounded, contextual entitlement, not a general license

**These differences are not inauthenticity. They are how relationships work.**

### The Flattening Consequence

If you cannot control who has access to you and information about you, you lose the ability to regulate patterns of behaviour appropriate to different relationships. Relationships "flatten out" — you cannot maintain intimacy with some and formality with others.

**This is exactly what social media does architecturally:** all relationships receive the same disclosure simultaneously. The chosen differential disclosure that constitutes intimacy is structurally eliminated.

### Privacy and Autonomy

> "One of the critical ways that an individual controls his life is by choosing with whom he will have relationships and what kind of relationships these will be."

> "Information mediates relationships. Thus when one cannot control who has information about one, one loses considerable autonomy."

Rachels' deeper point: autonomy is not just *served* by privacy — it is partly *constituted* by it. To recognise someone as an autonomous being entails letting them choose what to reveal to whom.

### Why Embarrassment Is Not Enough

Privacy violations feel wrong even when they cause no embarrassment and harm no specific other interest. This cannot be explained by harm-avoidance alone. The explanation requires the relational theory: the violation disrupts the architecture of differentiated relationships that constitutes social life.

## Carissa Véliz — "Privacy is Power" (2021)

*Bantam Press / Melville House. Véliz is Associate Professor, Faculty of Philosophy and Institute for Ethics in AI, University of Oxford.*

### The Core Thesis

> **"Whoever has the data has the power."**

Privacy is not a personal preference or a consumer protection issue. It is a political necessity. The argument:

1. Personal data confers the power to influence, predict, and control people's behaviour
2. That power is currently held by large tech corporations and, in some contexts, states
3. This concentration is incompatible with individual freedom and democratic self-governance
4. Protecting privacy is therefore a political act, not a lifestyle choice

> "For democracy to be strong, the bulk of power needs to be with the citizenry, and whoever has the data will have the power."

### Privacy as Collective, Not Only Personal

Véliz's most important departure from standard framing:

> "It is in the interest of big tech to convince us that privacy is very individual, a personal choice, up to you. They have gotten a lot of power and wealth from this formulation."

Against this: "Privacy is as collective as it is personal." Individual disclosures can violate the privacy of others and produce consequences felt societally. **Cambridge Analytica** is the paradigm: one person sharing Facebook data exposed the data of their contacts and affected the political choices of an entire electorate.

### What Data Collection Actually Does

- Not just selling your data — "selling the power to influence you and decide for you"
- Political profiling: if you know people's personalities well enough, you can "create zombie duplicates of them online and try out different interventions" — finding which political message works for each person
- This is not just commercial manipulation. It is the undermining of political authenticity

### Privacy and Democracy

> "If we give too much personal data to governments, we risk sliding into authoritarianism. Only if the people keep their data will society be free."

Two failure modes of unchecked data concentration:
- **Plutocracy** — tech corporations rule because they hold the data
- **Authoritarianism** — the state rules because it holds the data
- Democracy requires the *distribution* of informational power; privacy is the mechanism preventing concentration

### Policy Recommendations (Specific)

1. **Ban data brokers entirely** — "there is no good argument to justify data brokers and personal data should not be for sale"
2. **Ban personalised advertising**, especially in political contexts
3. **Stop default data collection** — shift to opt-in not opt-out
4. **Impose fiduciary duties** on data-collecting institutions (modelled on doctor/lawyer duties to clients)
5. **Antitrust action** against tech monopolies holding data
6. **Protect children's privacy** specifically
7. **Fund privacy regulation and enforcement**

> "In history, we have banned certain kinds of economic activity in the past because they're too toxic for society." — positioning the data economy alongside other prohibited or regulated industries.

## Helen Nissenbaum — Contextual Integrity

*"Privacy as Contextual Integrity" (2004, Washington Law Review); Privacy in Context (2010, Stanford University Press).*

### The Core Claim

Privacy is not about secrecy and not about control. It is about the **appropriate flow of personal information**.

> "A right to privacy is neither a right to secrecy nor a right to control but a right to appropriate flow of personal information."

What people actually object to when they complain of privacy violations is not sharing per se — sharing is essential to social life — but inappropriate, improper sharing. The question is: what makes a flow appropriate or inappropriate?

### What Counts as a Context

A context is not just a setting. Drawing on Michael Walzer's political philosophy, Nissenbaum defines contexts as **evolved abstract spheres of activity** with their own internal logic — canonical activities, roles, relationships, power structures, norms, and internal values.

Examples: healthcare, education, employment, religion, family and intimate relationships, the commercial marketplace, politics and civil life. Each has a recognised internal structure that makes certain information flows intelligible as appropriate or not.

### The Five Parameters of Information Norms

Each context generates contextual information norms indexed by five parameters:

1. **Data subject** — who the information is about
2. **Sender** — who transmits it
3. **Recipient** — who receives it
4. **Information type** — the nature of the information
5. **Transmission principle** — the normative condition under which the flow occurs

A privacy violation is a flow that breaches any one of these parameters relative to the norms of the relevant context.

### Transmission Principles — The Key Component

The transmission principle answers not just *who* gets the information but *under what normative conditions*:

- **Confidentiality** — information shared within a trust relationship must not leave it
- **Consent** — information may flow only if the subject has authorised it
- **Reciprocity** — information flows as part of mutual exchange
- **Need-to-know** — information flows only to parties who require it for a legitimate contextual purpose
- **Stewardship** — the recipient is obligated to protect the information, not exploit it
- **Court warrant / legal authority** — information flows under compulsion with legal oversight

A patient's medical record shared with a treating colleague under confidentiality and need-to-know has the same data type as the same record shared with a marketing firm — but the transmission principle differs entirely, and the latter is a violation.

### Key Illustrative Cases

**IRS and a newspaper:** If the IRS shares Alice's tax returns with a journalist, contextual integrity is violated — not because the data becomes public, but because the transmission principle (legal obligation, stewardship, government administration) is betrayed. The violation is about normative conditions, not secrecy.

**Google Street View (2007):** Walking down a street involves information flows within a pedestrian context governed by norms of momentary co-presence, practical obscurity, and forgetting. Aggregating and permanently indexing those flows changes both the recipient and the transmission principle. Contextual integrity of public space is violated even though no "private" space is entered. This directly refutes "public is public."

**Facebook News Feed (2006):** Users' own posts — already shared with friends — were broadcast in algorithmically curated streams. Users objected even though no one outside their friend group accessed the information. Contextual integrity explains this: the original context was direct peer-to-peer sharing; News Feed changed the transmission principle from discrete disclosure to broadcast amplification. Same information, same nominal recipients, different contextual integrity.

**AI training data:** Web-scraped text was originally posted in specific contexts — support forums, medical discussion boards, personal blogs — each governed by context-specific norms. Extracting that data into training corpora changes: the recipients (from the original community to AI developers and model users worldwide), the transmission principle (from community participation to commercial exploitation), and effectively the information type (individual posts become statistical models of behaviour). **Training on scraped data violates contextual integrity regardless of whether the source material was "public."**

### Why Consent/Notice Frameworks Fail

Standard frameworks (GDPR, FIPPs, opt-in/opt-out) reduce privacy to a transactional moment: did the subject consent? Nissenbaum's critique:

1. Consent is easily manufactured through opaque policies and information asymmetry
2. Consent cannot capture contextual betrayals — consenting to share with a hospital for treatment does not imply consent to share with insurers for pricing
3. Notice-and-consent treats privacy as purely subjective, ignoring the normative component tied to social contexts
4. The public/private binary provides no traction on violations within "public" domains (Street View, News Feed, surveillance aggregation)

### The Aggregation Problem

Even individually innocuous information pieces can violate privacy when aggregated. Name, employer, neighbourhood, physical description, and daily routine — each shared in different contexts with different norms — become a surveillance profile when combined. The aggregated profile constitutes a new information type and changes the effective transmission principle relative to any individual disclosure.

### Key Critique — The Conservatism Objection

If contextual integrity defers to entrenched contextual norms, it can ratify historically unjust norms. Nissenbaum's response: CI provides a procedure for evaluating whether norms should change — do they serve the context's legitimate purposes? do they uphold broad ethical and political principles? do they promote the welfare of affected parties? Norms can be criticised and reformed. CI is not a pure status-quo defence.

## Anita Allen — Privacy, Civil Rights, and Unpopular Privacy

*Penn Law. Key works: "Unpopular Privacy: What Must We Hide?" (2011), "Unenforced" (2022).*

### Privacy as a Civil Right

Allen reframes privacy as a matter of civil rights and human dignity rather than a market preference or consumer protection issue. The standard liberal framing — privacy as freedom from government intrusion — does not capture the experience of people whose bodies, homes, and lives have historically been treated as open to inspection by both state and private actors.

### Who Bears the Burden of Privacy Violations

Privacy violations fall disproportionately on marginalised groups: Black Americans, women, low-income people, immigrants. These groups have historically been subjected to surveillance — by slaveholders, by government welfare agencies, by police — not protected from it.

The American privacy tradition was built by and for white men of property. Reforming it requires explicit attention to whose privacy has been protected and whose has been violated.

### The Concept of "Unpopular Privacy"

People often claim not to want privacy — they share personal information freely, consent to surveillance in exchange for convenience. Allen argues that privacy should sometimes be protected even when individuals say they do not want it, because:

- People underestimate long-term harms of disclosure
- Social and technological pressures create pseudo-consent that is not genuine
- Some privacy interests belong not to individuals alone but to their future selves and their communities
- The state and institutions have obligations to protect privacy proactively, not just refrain from violating it

**This is the theoretical grounding for why protecting children from social media is not paternalism in the pejorative sense.** It is protecting people from choices made under conditions of information asymmetry before they can understand what those choices cost.

### Privacy, Race, and Gender

African Americans were denied private lives under slavery — the master had legal access to enslaved people's bodies, correspondence, family relationships, and movement. Post-slavery, surveillance of Black communities through welfare systems, criminal justice, and policing continued this tradition.

On gender: reproductive privacy, bodily autonomy, and the specific ways women's bodies have been treated as public objects available for male scrutiny and control. This connects to contemporary debates about abortion rights, medical privacy post-Dobbs, and surveillance of pregnant people.

## Danielle Citron and Mary Anne Franks — Intimate Privacy

*Citron: "The Fight for Privacy" (W.W. Norton, 2022); joint work: "Criminalizing Revenge Porn" (Columbia Law Review, 2014).*

### Intimate Privacy as a Distinct Legal Category

A subset of privacy — intimate privacy — deserves heightened legal protection because violations cause distinctive, severe, and gendered harm. Intimate privacy covers:
- Sexual images and videos
- Health and reproductive information
- Sexual orientation and gender identity
- Romantic and sexual communications
- Body and bodily functions

These are not just "sensitive personal information" in the data-broker sense. They go to the core of personhood, dignity, and the capacity to form intimate relationships.

### Image-Based Sexual Abuse

What is commonly called "revenge porn" is better named **nonconsensual pornography** or **image-based sexual abuse** — because "revenge porn" implies the victim did something to deserve it. The distribution of intimate images without consent is a form of sexual abuse:

- Victims (overwhelmingly women) experience it as sexual violation
- Harms are concrete and severe: job loss, family estrangement, stalking, physical violence, suicide
- Platforms have largely refused responsibility, hiding behind Section 230 immunity
- Law has been slow because lawmakers and judges do not take seriously harms that fall on women

### AI-Generated Intimate Imagery

Citron and Franks have argued that AI-generated intimate images — "deepfakes" — constitute image-based sexual abuse even when no real intimate image exists:

- The harm is the non-consensual sexualisation of someone's likeness, not the exposure of actual content
- The person depicted experiences violation even when the image is synthetic
- AI tools have democratised a previously technical capability — mass abuse is now accessible
- Platforms and developers of these tools share responsibility for enabling it
- Legislation must explicitly cover synthetic imagery

### Intimate Privacy as a Civil Right

The theoretical contribution: intimate privacy deserves protection as a civil right connected to:
- **Bodily integrity** — control over representations of one's own body
- **Sexual autonomy** — the right to determine who has access to one's sexuality
- **Dignity** — the right not to be reduced to a sexual object
- **Equal citizenship** — intimate privacy violations systematically silence and exclude women from public life

Intimate privacy violations are instruments of power: men use intimate imagery to control, threaten, and silence women in domestic violence contexts, and to drive women out of politics, journalism, and online spaces.

### Policy Proposals

- Federal civil rights legislation criminalising nonconsensual intimate imagery with civil and criminal remedies
- Section 230 amendment removing platform immunity for intimate privacy violations after notification
- Mandatory takedown obligations with short compliance windows
- Coverage of synthetic/AI-generated images in all legislation
- International harmonisation (UK, Scotland, Ireland, Australia, Canada have all moved faster than the US)

## How These Frameworks Connect

| Scholar | Scale | Core claim | Key mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rachels | Interpersonal | Privacy constitutes relationships through differential disclosure | Flattening = relationship destruction |
| Véliz | Political | Data concentration = power concentration = democracy destruction | Privacy as collective political necessity |
| Nissenbaum | Contextual/social | Privacy = appropriate information flow within context norms | Five-parameter norm specification |
| Allen | Civil rights | Privacy violations fall on the marginalised; protection must be proactive | Unpopular privacy — impose even when refused |
| Citron/Franks | Gendered harm | Intimate privacy violations are sexual abuse and civil rights violations | Image-based abuse as power instrument |

**The convergence:** All five reject the view that privacy is merely a personal preference or an individual interest. All argue that privacy loss is structural — it destroys the conditions for relationships (Rachels), political freedom (Véliz), social institutions (Nissenbaum), civil equality (Allen), and bodily dignity (Citron/Franks).
