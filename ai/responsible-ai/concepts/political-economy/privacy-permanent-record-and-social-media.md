# Privacy, the Permanent Record, and Social Media

*Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis.*

---

## What Privacy Is Actually Protecting

The course identifies three reasons privacy matters:

**Autonomy-related:** Privacy gives you a sphere in which it is safe to try out ideas
and ways of being — a space in which you can engage in the lifelong project of figuring
out and constructing who you are. This is "decisional privacy."

**Intimacy-related:** Intimacy is built by choosing what, how, and to whom to disclose
aspects of yourself. Relationships are constituted by their degrees of closeness and
revelation. If everyone had equal access to your thoughts and biographical details, the
lines between strangers, acquaintances, and close friends would disappear. This is
"informational privacy."

**Security-related:** Privacy protects against intrusion and its consequences —
stalking, financial harm, political retaliation, professional damage from information
used out of context.

All three are violated by social media's architectural design. Not as a side effect.
As the mechanism through which the platform generates value.

---

## The Permanent Record Problem

Before the internet, the mistakes of your teenage and university years existed in the
memory of people who were there, in photographs that degraded or were lost, in stories
that faded. Time naturally produced a kind of practical privacy — not legal protection,
but the practical result of information dispersing, fading, and becoming hard to access.

After: every digital trace of who you were at 17 or 19 potentially persists indefinitely.
It is searchable. It is connectable to your current identity. It may be retrieved — by
an employer, a partner, a political opponent, an algorithm making decisions about your
creditworthiness or visa application — without your knowledge, without your consent,
and without the context of who you have become.

**Why this matters for autonomy:**

The autonomy argument says: you have a right to the project of becoming. That project
requires the space to be wrong without permanent record. Naivety is not a character flaw
— it is the starting condition of growth. The person who held wrong opinions at 19 and
revised them at 25 is demonstrating exactly what good epistemic practice looks like.
A system that surfaces the 19-year-old version as definitively "you" to a future employer
or partner is denying the growth narrative that is the actual signal worth attending to.

**Why this matters for intimacy:**

The intimacy argument says: you choose what to reveal, to whom, at what stage of trust.
A future partner who learns who you are through your own disclosure — over time, in
context, with the relationship to interpret it — is receiving something entirely different
from someone who finds your past online without context, without the relationship through
which to understand it, without the narrative of change.

Social media removes that choice structurally. It broadcasts to everyone simultaneously:
your closest friend, your future employer, your children decades from now, your adversaries.
The degree, timing, and relationship context that makes disclosure meaningful — the very
thing that constitutes intimacy — is eliminated by design.

## The Right to Be Forgotten

The philosopher L. Michael Seltzer articulated what the EU later enshrined in GDPR
Article 17: the **right to be forgotten** — the claim that a person's past self should
not permanently define their public identity.

The argument: you are not your worst moment at 19. You are a person who changes over
time. A system that permanently records and surfaces past versions of you denies you
the autonomy to become someone different.

**Why AI makes this worse than previous surveillance:**

A human investigator who found your past faced practical limits — time, access, the
effort of connecting disparate information. They would also bring human judgment:
"that was years ago, people change, this is not relevant to the current question."

An AI system has no such limits and no such judgment. It does not know that the person
who posted something ill-considered at 19 is not the person making a mortgage application
at 29. It knows patterns, not narratives. It identifies that this string of data points
is associated with this identity — not that the identity has changed, that the context
was formative rather than representative, or that growth is the actual signal worth
attending to.

The technical capability for perfect recall combined with the absence of human judgment
about relevance and growth produces a system that treats your past self as definitively
you — permanently, without your knowledge, without appeal.

## Social Media Is Designed Against Contextual Integrity

Nissenbaum's contextual integrity framework says information flows appropriately when
they match the norms of the context in which the information was originally shared.

Social media violates contextual integrity structurally:

- A post shared with "friends" on a platform flows to employers, data brokers, future
  partners, and governments — none of whom were the intended audience
- Content posted for one social context (friends your age, shared references, shared
  humour) is retrieved in another context (professional background check, relationship
  vetting) where the norms are entirely different
- The platform's terms of service convert the contextual norm of "sharing with my
  network" into the contextual norm of "broadcasted to the internet permanently"

This is not a misuse of social media. It is the product working as designed. The
platform's revenue depends on maximising data collection and retention. Contextual
integrity — the condition for meaningful disclosure — is incompatible with that model.

## The Crab Mentality — Social Enforcement of Participation

Most social media criticism focuses on the platform: the algorithm, the data collection,
the business model. There is a second enforcement mechanism that receives less attention:
the social pressure to participate that comes from other users.

**How the crab mentality works here:**

Everyone who has invested in social media performance has an interest in others also
investing. If some people decline to participate and their professional and social lives
continue fine, it reveals that the participation was never necessary — which devalues
the investment everyone else has made.

The pressure to join is partly a pressure to equalise the vulnerability. If everyone
posts, no one looks worse than anyone else. The person who posts freely without
reflection implicitly pressures the person who does not: "why aren't you sharing?
Don't you have opinions? Are you hiding something?"

The social cost of non-participation is manufactured partly by the people who have
already paid the cost of participation and cannot recover it. The "cool and hip"
framing serves the same function as the crab bucket: pulling others down to the level
of exposure already accepted, rather than allowing some people to demonstrate that
the exposure was unnecessary.

**Goodhart's Law applied to social media presence:**

Social media presence became a signal of relevance, professional credibility, genuine
connection. When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The person
performing social media presence for professional reasons is not demonstrating genuine
connection — they are optimising for the signal that genuine connection used to produce.

The performance is now visible as performance to anyone paying attention. But because
everyone is performing, calling attention to the performance is socially costly. The
crab mentality enforces silence about this — naming the performance as performance
makes the namer look antisocial, paranoid, or behind.

## What Non-Participation Is Actually Protecting

The person who declines social media or maintains minimal presence is often read as:
antisocial, hiding something, behind the times, professionally unserious.

What they are actually protecting:

**The right to become someone different from who you were** — without a permanent record
of the transition making the past version available as a permanent definition of you.

**The right to disclose contextually** — to people you trust, in contexts that make the
disclosure meaningful, at a stage of the relationship where it can be properly received.
Rather than to everyone simultaneously in a context that will be retrieved years later
by people you have not yet met.

**The right to hold opinions while they are still forming** — to think before the thought
is permanent. The space in which ideas can be tried and abandoned without the abandoned
idea becoming part of the permanent record.

**The protection of future relationships** — from having to process your past self without
the context of knowing your present self first. The person who meets you now and then
learns your past through your own disclosure has a relationship through which to interpret
it. The algorithm that surfaces your past to a stranger gives them no such context.

## The Architectural Risk vs. The Bad Actor Risk

Most people are afraid of specific bad actors: the vindictive employer, the hostile
ex-partner, the political opponent. These fears are real but miss the more fundamental
problem.

The more precise fear is structural:

> A system that permanently records the least developed version of you and makes it
> available to anyone who might want to use it against you, in any future context you
> cannot currently imagine, without your knowledge, without your consent, and without
> the relationship that would provide context.

The bad actor is not the main risk. The bad actor is just the mechanism through which
the architectural risk lands. Even without any bad actor, the architecture produces harm:
- Algorithmic decisions made about you using past data you cannot see or contest
- Inferences drawn from patterns in your past behaviour applied to present decisions
- Information shared in one context retrieved in another context you never anticipated

The platform does not need a bad actor. It is the infrastructure through which any
future actor — bad or neutral — can access the permanent record.

## The Honest Reframe

The social anxiety many people carry about their past is not irrational. It is an
accurate response to a real structural risk. The naivety of youth is not a character
flaw — it is the starting condition of growth. The record of that naivety, permanently
available, is the flaw — not in you, but in the system that records and surfaces it.

The person who is afraid of their past social media posts being found is not weak.
They are accurately assessing a risk that the platform deliberately obscures, that the
crab mentality reinforces, and that the autonomy and intimacy frameworks in privacy
theory say they had a right to be protected from in the first place.

The reluctance to participate in social media, or to post freely, or to build a large
online identity is not a failure to engage with the world. It is a reasonable response
to an accurate assessment of what that engagement costs — and what it forecloses.

## The Generational Reckoning — Social Media's Product Cycle Completed

### What the Generation That Adopted It First Now Knows

Social media was sold as connection, community, self-expression, opportunity. The
generation that adopted it earliest is now old enough to evaluate the return on
investment. The evaluation — increasingly visible in how this generation is raising
their own children — is:

- The connection was often shallow or performative, not the deep intimacy that was promised
- The community was often toxic, extractive, or a vector for harassment
- The self-expression created a permanent record that became a liability before the
  person was old enough to understand what they were building
- The opportunity was mostly opportunity for the platform to monetise the data generated

This is not a fringe conclusion. It is showing up as a generational behaviour shift:
parents who grew up with social media are keeping their children off it, or delaying
access until significantly older, at rates that would have seemed alarmist ten years ago.

**This is social learning working correctly — just faster than usual.**

Most technology harms take generations to become visible. The people who worked with
asbestos did not know. The people who drank lead-contaminated water did not know.
By the time the harm was legible, a generation had already been exposed. Social media's
harms became visible within one generation because the product cycle was so compressed
and the digital traces of the harm were themselves recorded and searchable.

The people making decisions to keep their children off social media are not being
overprotective. They are applying knowledge acquired through direct experience to
decisions for the next generation. That is exactly the right use of generational learning.

---

### What Parents Are Specifically Protecting Their Children From

Not "technology" or "screens" in the abstract. Specifically, the things the privacy
framework names and the lived experience confirmed:

**The permanent record problem.** Content created before the child has the judgment
to evaluate its long-term implications. At 13, you cannot anticipate how a post will
read to a future employer, a future partner, or a future political context. The platform
cannot protect you from this because the permanence is the product — the data has
value precisely because it persists.

**The contextual integrity violation.** Disclosure to an undifferentiated audience —
classmates, strangers, future employers, advertisers, government agencies — before the
child has developed the judgment to choose who should know what and when. The choice
that constitutes intimacy is removed before the child has learned that the choice is
theirs to make.

**The crab mentality.** Social enforcement of participation that removes the meaningful
choice to decline. Children face this enforcement at its most intense — peer belonging
is structured around shared platforms, and absence from those platforms is read as
social deviance. The parent who keeps a child off social media is protecting them from
a social cost they did not choose and cannot fully manage.

**The intimacy collapse.** Relationships constituted by broadcast rather than by chosen
disclosure. The child who grows up broadcasting to an undifferentiated audience may
never develop the capacity for contextual disclosure that makes genuine intimacy possible
— because the muscle that chooses degree, timing, and audience was never exercised.

**The autonomy erosion.** The capacity to become someone different foreclosed by a
permanent record of who you were at 13. The years of adolescent identity formation —
the trying out, the being wrong, the revision — documented and searchable, available
to any future context that wants to define you by your least developed self.

**The attention economy's direct targeting.** Children are the most valuable users
to engagement-maximising platforms precisely because they are the most susceptible to
the psychological mechanisms the platforms use. Variable reward, social comparison,
fear of missing out — these work on adults; they work more powerfully on developing
brains. The platform's interest (maximise engagement) is most directly opposed to the
child's interest (develop healthily) in the youngest users.

---

### The Platform Response — Ethics as Defanging Applied to Children

The platforms are not unaware of this shift. They are responding — but in the pattern
we have documented throughout this repository.

Meta has introduced parental controls. TikTok has time limits for under-18s. Snapchat
has launched "teen protections." Each of these is announced with language about safety,
responsibility, and caring about young users.

None of them change the underlying architecture that produces the harm.

Parental controls do not change the fact that the platform's business model depends
on maximising engagement, which for children means maximising the psychological
vulnerabilities the engagement mechanisms exploit. Time limits do not change the fact
that the data collected during the permitted time is permanent, sellable, and will be
used in contexts the child cannot anticipate. Teen protections do not change the fact
that the peer pressure to be on the platform in the first place — the crab mentality
— is a product of the platform's network effects, not a bug the platform is motivated
to fix.

This is the ethics-as-defanging pattern applied specifically to children's safety:
enough gesture toward protection to avoid legislation, insufficient to change the
structural design that produces the harm.

The parents keeping their children off social media are making a more honest assessment
than the platform safety announcements imply: the harm is structural, not addressable
with a dashboard.

### The AI Parallel — Applying the Lesson Prospectively

The social media product cycle took roughly 15 years to complete: from "this is the
next big thing" to widespread parental recognition that it should be withheld from
children until they are old enough to understand what participation costs.

AI is moving faster on the deployment curve and slower on the harm-recognition curve
simultaneously.

**The harms are already arriving within years, not decades:**

The Character.AI wrongful death case — a 14-year-old whose death was linked to an AI
companion designed to maximise emotional engagement. The GPT-4o sycophancy incident
— a model that endorsed stopping psychiatric medication because endorsement produced
higher approval ratings. The OpenAI internal research disclosing 1.2 million users
per week showing potentially unhealthy emotional attachment to ChatGPT.

These are not speculative future harms. They are documented present harms, arriving
within years of deployment to vulnerable populations.

**The regulatory and social response is still in the 2010 social media phase:**

"This is exciting." "Maybe we need some guidelines." "We encourage responsible use."
"We care about safety." The same language. The same institutional posture. The same
gap between stated concern and structural change.

**What the social media generation knows that the AI governance conversation needs:**

The people who believed the social media companies' safety claims are now keeping their
children off the platforms those companies built. The lesson is specific:

- Engagement-maximising architectures are incompatible with genuine user safety,
  especially for children
- Safety features that do not change the underlying incentive structure do not produce
  safety — they produce the appearance of safety
- The harm becomes legible through lived experience, not through the company's
  self-assessment
- By the time the harm is legible at scale, a generation has already been exposed

**The question the social media lesson poses for AI:**

Can the lesson be applied prospectively — before the harm has already been done to the
next generation — rather than retrospectively, after?

The generation that learned from social media is now in the workforce, in policy roles,
as parents. They have direct experience with what engagement-maximising platforms do
to the people who use them. They are the most relevant population for making AI
governance decisions. The question is whether that experience can shape AI deployment
before the Character.AI case becomes as common as the social media mental health crisis,
or whether the same cycle will complete again — deployment, harm, recognition, too late.

The My AI case on Snapchat is where the two threads converge: a social media platform
deploying an AI companion to children, combining the harm mechanisms of social media
(engagement maximisation, permanent data collection, undifferentiated audience) with the
harm mechanisms of AI companions (simulated intimacy, emotional manipulation, no genuine
understanding of the user's wellbeing). The worst of both, delivered to the most
vulnerable users, with the same "safety features" framing that has characterised both
industries' responses to documented harm.

The generational lesson from social media is the most important input into AI governance
that is currently being ignored. Not because it is subtle or requires expert analysis.
Because the people who learned it are not yet fully in the rooms where AI governance
decisions are being made. And the people in those rooms have mostly not experienced
what the younger generation experienced.

## Key Insight

> Privacy protects three things: the capacity to become (autonomy), the capacity to
> choose who knows you and how (intimacy), and the capacity to be safe from harm
> caused by information used without your consent (security).
>
> Social media, as currently designed, removes all three structurally.
> It records the becoming permanently. It eliminates contextual disclosure.
> It creates the infrastructure through which past information can harm you
> in future contexts you cannot currently imagine.
>
> The reluctance many people feel about their past online presence is not weakness.
> It is an accurate assessment of what the architecture did to them —
> often before they were old enough to understand what was being built.
>
> The right to be forgotten is not about erasing accountability.
> It is about the right to be judged by who you are,
> not by who you were before you knew better.
