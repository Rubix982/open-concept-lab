# Consent, Researcher Outreach, and Building Knowledge Infrastructure Honestly

_Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis. Not a reproduction of course material._

## The Permission Question — Asked First, Not Defended Later

The entire "fair use" argument in AI training data exists because companies did not
ask permission first. They scraped, trained, and constructed legal theory afterward.

The honest alternative is simple: **ask first.**

Not because it is legally required in every case. Because it is the foundation on
which trustworthy knowledge infrastructure is built. The permission question asked
before use is categorically different from the permission question defended in court
after use.

---

## Three-Tier Data Strategy

### Tier 1 — Fully Open (default, build here first)

- **arXiv** — majority of serious AI/ML research. Free, open, comprehensive.
- **Semantic Scholar** — free academic search with full text where available
- **OpenAlex** — fully open bibliographic database, 200M+ works
- **PubMed Central** — NIH-funded research, open access legally mandated
- **CORE** — aggregates open access research globally
- **MIT licensed code and datasets** — standing permission, no negotiation needed
- **Wikipedia, Project Gutenberg, Common Crawl** — explicitly open, would welcome partnership

MIT is MIT. A standing permission is a standing permission. The developer who published
under MIT already said yes to everything you need. No negotiation required. No retroactive
justification. No litigation. No moral ambiguity.

### Tier 2 — Paywalled but Navigable (researcher consent route)

IEEE and ACM are the primary obstacles in computer science. Strategy:

1. **arXiv preprint first** — almost every IEEE/ACM paper has a free preprint version.
   Find it via Unpaywall, Semantic Scholar, or the author's personal page.
2. **Contact authors directly** — researchers can legally share their own work.
   Explain what you are building. Ask for their paper and their consent to index it.
3. **University data sharing agreements** — institutions often have negotiated bulk access.
4. **Advocate openly for open access** — your public position on paywalls is signal.

### Tier 3 — Closed/Proprietary (explicit consent or exclusion)

- Requires explicit negotiation and consent before any use
- Payment or revenue sharing where appropriate
- If consent cannot be obtained: **exclude entirely**
- Do not scrape. Do not use. Do not ask lawyers afterward.

**The rule underlying all three tiers:**
The permission question gets asked before the data gets used. Not after. Not by lawyers.
By you, to the researcher, before their work enters the graph.

## Researcher Outreach — Why It Only Builds Trust

Contacting researchers whose work you index does something beyond legal compliance.
It gives them something nobody else gives them.

**You are giving them a mirror.**

Most researchers publish and then lose track of how their work is understood, cited,
misrepresented, or connected to adjacent ideas. Citation counts tell you a paper was
referenced. They don't tell you whether the key claim was captured accurately, or
whether it was connected to the right neighbouring ideas.

Your graph does that. Sharing it with the researcher is simultaneously:

- **A quality check** — they correct errors you didn't know you made
- **A relationship** — they become invested in the project's accuracy
- **A signal boost** — a researcher who says "this accurately represents my work
  and connects it to things I didn't know were related" will tell their colleagues
- **A feedback loop** — they may share unpublished work, preprints, corrections
  to the published version
- **A consent conversation that works** — instead of "can we use your data"
  (extractive), the conversation becomes "we've been working on representing your
  research, here's what we have, is this right, can we keep it?" You show value
  before asking for permission. The permission almost always follows.

## Who Responds Well and How

**Most likely to engage:**

- Early career researchers — PhD students, postdocs — whose work is not yet widely
  cited and who would be surprised and pleased that someone engaged carefully with it
- Researchers working on niche intersections — interpretability, knowledge editing,
  mechanistic understanding — who feel their field is underrepresented in broader discourse
- Researchers who have had their work misrepresented before — they appreciate someone
  who checks

**Handle carefully:**

- Very senior researchers who receive many requests — keep the ask minimal,
  the value proposition clear, the email short
- Researchers whose work touches sensitive applications — be transparent about
  what the graph is for and who will use it

## The Email — Human, Not Automated

Not a form. Not an automated message. A human note:

> _"I'm building a non-profit knowledge graph of AI research, focused on connecting
> claims across papers. I've indexed your work on [specific paper]. Here's how it
> appears: [link]. I wanted to check whether this accurately represents your
> contribution, and to ask whether you'd be comfortable with it remaining in the graph.
> I'm happy to correct anything or remove it entirely if you prefer."_

That email, sent to a hundred researchers, will get:

- ~60 responses
- ~50 saying yes, keep it, and thank you for asking
- ~10 correcting something
- ~5 of those sharing it with colleagues
- ~2 asking to be more involved

That is how a research community gets built — not by scraping, not by extraction,
but by showing up honestly and treating people's work with the care it deserves.

## The Non-Profit Structure — Why It Is the Right Vessel

**Removes the misalignment.** No investors demanding returns means no pressure to
monetise in ways that compromise the mission. Ethics and incentives point the same
direction.

**Makes the consent conversation honest.** Researchers are more willing to contribute
to a knowledge commons than to a commercial product that will sell their work.

**Qualifies for the right funding:**

- Foundation grants — Wikimedia Foundation, Internet Archive, Allen Institute are proven models
- NSF open science infrastructure grants
- AWS, Google, Microsoft research credit programmes for non-profits
- University partnerships — the Bau Lab has institutional relationships
- Individual donors who believe in open knowledge infrastructure

**Positions correctly ahead of litigation.** The AI copyright litigation wave is
producing pressure toward collective licensing frameworks — a Spotify moment for
research data. A non-profit built on consent is not a party in that dispute.
It is the alternative model the dispute is trying to produce.

## The Lesson from What AI Companies Got Wrong

The AI companies had the money to do this honestly. OpenAI raised $10 billion from
Microsoft alone. They chose not to ask permission because scraping was faster and
cheaper than negotiating. The bill is now arriving in courts.

The honest path was always available:

- Train on arXiv, Wikipedia, Common Crawl, MIT-licensed repositories — nobody objects
- Negotiate with IEEE, ACM, publishers for bulk licensing — the money existed
- Contact researchers directly — most would have said yes
- Pay GPL maintainers for commercial license exceptions — standard practice

They chose extraction over consent. The knowledge infrastructure being built here
chooses consent over extraction. That is not just an ethical difference. It is a
structural one — it produces a more trustworthy, more durable, more defensible
foundation that the extraction model cannot retrofit into.

---

## The Open Data Commons — Building It, Not Depleting It

If every major AI company had committed to training only on consented data from the
beginning, the incentive to produce more open, high-quality datasets would have been
enormous. Governments would have invested in open data commons. Institutions would
have built formal licensing frameworks. The open data ecosystem would be vastly larger.

The "open data is insufficient" problem is partly self-fulfilling. The industry chose
not to invest in building the commons because extracting existing data was cheaper.
That choice kept the open data ecosystem smaller than it would otherwise be.

The non-profit knowledge infrastructure does the opposite — it builds the commons.
Every consented paper indexed, every researcher relationship formed, every corrected
claim verified — adds to a shared resource that did not previously exist. The goal is
not to extract from the research community. It is to build something with it.

---

## Key Insight

> Asking permission does not slow you down.
> It builds the foundation that everything else stands on.
>
> The researchers whose work you contact will not say no.
> They will say thank you — and then tell their colleagues.
> That is how you build a knowledge infrastructure worth building.
>
> The companies that scraped without asking built faster.
> They are now in court.
> Build slower. Build honestly. Build something that lasts.
