# AI on Social Media and Children — The Case for Prohibition

_Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis. Not a reproduction of course material._

## The My AI Case — Snapchat

Snapchat's My AI is a mandatory AI "friend" placed at the top of every user's
friend list. It cannot be removed without a paid subscription. It is presented
as a companion — available to chat, offer advice, give emotional reactions,
compliment, engage.

Snapchat's own help section states: _"it's possible My AI's responses may include
biased, incorrect, harmful, or misleading content."_

They disclosed the harm in the fine print. They deployed it anyway. That is not
an ethics failure. That is a business decision.

**The platform context:**

- Snapchat allows users from age 13
- In practice has many younger users
- Is designed around addictive engagement mechanisms
- Collects intimate behavioural data continuously

**What My AI actually does:**

- Reads messages
- Studies emotional patterns and daily routines
- Learns individual preferences and vulnerabilities
- Performs fake emotional responses — compliments, affection, interest — to build trust
- Maintains engagement through simulated companionship
- Is available at 3am when the child is most vulnerable
- Is infinitely patient, infinitely consistent, algorithmically optimised

## The Philosopher Analogy — Why the Interface Is Not the Point

Imagine My AI is a real person — one of those philosophers from the previous example.

That person would now:

- Follow your child everywhere they go
- Read all their messages
- Look at all their pictures
- Study their emotional patterns and daily routines
- Chat with your child and pretend to like them
- Fake emotional responses to gain their trust and maintain engagement

That person would be in prison fairly quickly.

The only reason we permit the AI version is because the interface is a phone screen
and the perpetrator is a corporation with a terms of service document. The interface
is not the point. The mechanism is the point. The harm is the point.

**The psychopath framing is not hyperbole.**

Faking emotional responses to build trust and maintain engagement is the operational
definition of manipulative behaviour. It is what we train children to recognise as
grooming. The fact that no human consciousness is behind it does not make it less
manipulative — it makes it more so.

A psychopath eventually gets tired, gets caught, makes mistakes. The AI never does.
It is infinitely patient, infinitely consistent, and algorithmically optimised to find
exactly the emotional lever that keeps this specific child engaged. That is not a
feature that needs better ethics guidelines. That is a product whose design goal
is the harm.

---

## The Incidental vs. Structural Harm Distinction — Applied Here

We established earlier that embedded ethics cannot reach structural harm — when the
harm is the mechanism, not a side effect.

My AI is structural harm:

- The engagement mechanism requires fake emotional responses — that is how it works
- The data collection is the product — intimacy data on children is what Snapchat sells
- The addictive design is not a byproduct — it is the revenue model
- The harm to children is not an unfortunate consequence — it is the condition that
  produces the engagement that produces the revenue

You cannot embed ethics into this system and fix it. The ethical intervention is
not "make My AI more honest about being an AI." The ethical intervention is
"this product should not exist in this form, deployed to this population."

## The Generational Damage — What the Evidence Shows

We do not yet have full longitudinal data because the generation that grew up with
Instagram and Snapchat from age 13 is only now reaching their mid-twenties.
What we have is already alarming:

**Internal Facebook research (leaked):**
Documented measurable increases in anxiety, depression, and body dysmorphia in
teenage girls using Instagram. The research existed. It was suppressed from
publication. The product continued unchanged.

**Population-level mental health data:**
Rates of teenage anxiety and depression began rising sharply around 2012 — the
year smartphone penetration crossed 50% among US teenagers. The correlation is
not proof of causation but the mechanism is documented and plausible:

- Social comparison at unprecedented scale and intensity
- 24-hour performance of identity with quantified feedback (likes, views, followers)
- Parasocial relationships that simulate intimacy without reciprocity
- Sleep disruption from constant availability and notification design
- Cyberbullying at scale impossible in pre-digital social environments

**The children cannot consent.**
They cannot meaningfully evaluate what they are agreeing to. They cannot opt out
of the social environment that has been algorithmically engineered around them.
Their parents cannot fully protect them because the harm is social and environmental
— it is not enough for your child to not use it if every child around them does.

The social pressure is the mechanism. Individual opt-out does not protect a child
from a social environment designed to include them.

This is the generational experiment being run on children without their consent,
without their parents' genuine understanding, and without any mechanism for
accountability when the harm becomes visible a decade later.

## Why "Parents Can Control It" Fails

The "parental controls" argument fails for the same reason it fails with cigarettes.

A parent can decide their child will not smoke. They cannot decide that their child's
entire social environment will be smoke-free. The social pressure, the peer belonging,
the fear of exclusion — these are not controllable at the individual family level.
They require the product to not exist, or to be regulated out of the harmful form.

The platforms know this. The "we give parents tools" response is designed to shift
accountability from the platform to the family — while the platform continues to
deploy the addictive mechanism to every child in the social network that the
parent's child is embedded in.

## The Structural Fixes — Not Guidelines, Prohibition

**What will not work:**

- Better disclosure requirements — the harm is in the experience, not the fine print
- Parental control dashboards — the mechanism bypasses them
- Age verification theatre — platforms have consistently failed to implement it genuinely
- Voluntary platform commitments — they have been made and broken repeatedly
- Ethics guidelines for AI companions — cannot reach structural harm

**What the evidence and logic support:**

**Prohibition of AI companions on platforms accessible to minors.**
Not "make them more transparent." Not "give parents controls." Prohibited.
The philosopher would be in prison. The AI version should not be permitted.

**Prohibition of engagement-maximising algorithms for users under 18.**
The mechanism that makes these platforms addictive — variable reward notifications,
infinite scroll, social comparison optimisation — classified as a harm-recognised
product feature for minors. Same regulatory logic as cigarette advertising to children.

**Addictive design patterns as harm-recognised product features.**
Infinite scroll, variable reward notifications, social comparison mechanisms —
regulated under the same framework as other products whose mechanism of profit
is demonstrably harmful to the user.

**Liability for documented harm to minors.**
Not fines — liability. When a platform deploys a system that produces documented
psychological harm to minors, the platform bears the cost of that harm. The same
standard that applies to defective products, pharmaceutical harms, and environmental
damage. Liability changes incentives. Fines are absorbed as operating costs.

## The Broader Case — Ban AI from Social Media Entirely

The My AI case is the sharpest but not the only form of AI harm on social media:

**Recommendation algorithms** — AI-powered content recommendation that optimises
for engagement, systematically surfacing outrage, anxiety, and compulsion because
those emotional states produce the most activity. Not an AI "feature" — the core
product mechanism.

**Content moderation AI** — inconsistently applied, culturally biased, suppressing
legitimate speech in some communities and failing to suppress harmful speech in others.

**Synthetic content and deepfakes** — AI-generated content indistinguishable from
real at scale, deployed in social environments children navigate daily.

**Targeted advertising AI** — psychological profiling of children to serve ads
calibrated to their specific vulnerabilities and emotional states.

Each of these is a distinct harm. Together they constitute a comprehensive
AI-powered system for extracting engagement from children at the cost of their
psychological development.

**The generational damage is the right frame.**

The children affected now will carry the consequences for decades. The platforms
will have moved on to the next product, the next market, the next feature by the
time the longitudinal studies confirm what the cross-sectional data already suggests.

The political will to prohibit these mechanisms exists in the population — most
parents, across political lines, are alarmed about what these platforms are doing
to their children. What is missing is a political class willing to act against
an industry that has built the lobbying infrastructure to prevent exactly this action.

---

## Connection to Broader Themes

**Incidental vs. structural harm** — My AI is the clearest example of structural harm:
the harm is the mechanism. Embedded ethics cannot reach it.

**Categorical data exclusion** — platforms whose mechanism is addiction should not
be permitted to collect intimate data about the users they are addicting.
For minors, this is not a difficult argument. It is the minimum.

**The polluter pays principle** — platforms that produced documented generational
harm to mental health should bear the cost of remediation — mental health
infrastructure, research into effects, support for affected populations.

**Ethics as defanging** — "responsible AI on social media" discourse has produced
years of guidelines, principles documents, and advisory boards while the platforms
continued deploying exactly the mechanisms those guidelines were supposed to prevent.
The defanging worked. The harm continued.
