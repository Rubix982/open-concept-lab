# A People's Guide to AI — Notes

*Mimi Onuoha and Mother Cyborg (Diana Nucera), 2018.
Open Society Foundation funded. Creative Commons licensed.
Full text: peoplesguidetoai.com*

---

## What Makes This Different

Most AI ethics discourse is written by people with institutional power,
for people with institutional power, about people who have neither.

This document inverts that. It is a community education tool written
specifically for people who are affected by AI but excluded from
conversations about it — Detroit residents without internet access,
undocumented immigrants, low-income communities. The exact
populations who face the greatest risk and have the least voice.

---

## Key Ideas Worth Keeping

**AI as salt, not a food group:**
"One way to think of AI is as salt rather than its own food group.
It's less interesting to consider it on its own. But once you add salt
to your food it can transform the meal." Not threatening, not
revolutionary — something that changes everything it touches, that
you should understand before it gets added to your food without
your knowledge.

**Data violence:**
"This overcollection of data about some people and undercollection
of data that serves those people results in a cycle of data violence."
Precise formulation: marginalised communities are simultaneously
over-surveilled (data extracted without consent, used against them
in predictive policing) and under-served (data that would help them
— crime against them, health outcomes, displacement — is not
collected). Both directions of the imbalance are active harms.

**The Za'irajah:**
The book traces the intellectual ancestry of computation to a
5th-century Islamic paper machine — disks with combinations of
number values associated with letters — that would take a question
as input and output a poem or riddle to inspire thought. From Ibn
Khaldun's Muqaddimah. A deliberate choice: the origin of the ideas
that produced AI belongs to a lineage that mainstream Western AI
history systematically erases.

**James Boggs, 1963:**
"Automation replaces men. What is new is that now, unlike most
earlier periods, the displaced men have nowhere to go." A Black
autoworker at Ford in Detroit writing about automation before
computers existed as we know them — predicting the exact shape of
the AI displacement problem sixty years before it arrived.

**The agency framing:**
"We are still at a moment where we can affect the trajectory of the
future if we are aware of what changes we seek and what we are
collectively working towards." Not grand claims — specific examples:
Mexican researchers using ML to find graves of desaparecidos,
Icelandic prosthetics that learn from amputee movement. AI serving
people outside the mainstream.
