# Employee Care and the Discretionary Investment Problem

*Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis.*

---

## The Standard Model and Why It Fails

The standard model of employee retention treats engagement as something you purchase
through compensation and retain through switching costs — vesting schedules, benefits
cliffs, pension entitlement. This model produces a specific behaviour: people stay.

It does not produce what organisations actually need from experienced employees:
the discretionary investment that grows with seniority.

Junior employee contribution is mostly defined by the task. The job description
specifies what is required. Success means doing those things adequately.

Senior employee contribution is mostly defined by what they choose to give beyond
the task:
- The institutional knowledge they share rather than guard
- The junior employees they develop because they care about the organisation's
  future, not just their current status
- The problems they surface before they become visible crises
- The relationships with clients, partners, and communities that belong to the
  relationship — not to the job title
- The culture they model for people who are watching what the senior person does
  when nobody is requiring them to do anything in particular
- The honest feedback they give upward — the concerns raised, the dissents registered,
  the "I think we're wrong about this" said in a room where that is uncomfortable

None of this is in the job description. None of it can be contractually required.
All of it depends on one thing: whether the employee believes, from accumulated
experience, that the organisation is genuinely on their side.

---

## What "Genuinely on Their Side" Actually Means

Not perfectly on their side. Not always right. Not without conflict. But genuinely
oriented toward their flourishing — treating them as people whose development,
circumstances, and aspirations matter, not just as inputs to be optimised.

The test is simple and observable over time: when the organisation's interest and
the employee's interest conflict, what happens? If the organisation consistently
chooses its own interest without acknowledgment, without explanation, without genuine
attempt to find a path that respects both — the relationship turns flat. The employee
continues to show up. The discretionary investment stops.

This is the "half-way relationship" that most workplaces produce. The form is there —
the salary, the meetings, the performance reviews, the benefits. The substance is
absent — the genuine curiosity about what this person needs to do their best work,
the honest feedback that treats them as capable of handling the truth, the sharing
of outcomes when the organisation does well because the employees worked hard.

## What Genuine Care Actually Requires

### Autonomy over meaningful work

People want to be responsible for something real, to have the authority to make
decisions within that domain, and to see the connection between their work and an
outcome that matters. Not unlimited freedom — trust commensurate with demonstrated
capability. The organisation that gives meaningful autonomy is communicating: I
believe in your judgment. That is not a perk. It is a form of respect that compounds.

### Honest feedback that treats people as capable of growth

The most common workplace failure is the feedback vacuum. People go months without
knowing whether they are doing well. Then they receive a performance review with no
relationship to any conversation they remember having.

Honest, specific, timely feedback — including the uncomfortable kind — is a form of
respect. It communicates: I take your development seriously enough to tell you the
truth. The manager who only says positive things is not protecting the employee —
they are denying the employee the information they need to grow. That is a form of
neglect dressed as kindness.

### Seeing the whole person, not just the role

What does this person actually need to do their best work? What are they trying to
build over the arc of their career, and is this organisation part of that journey?
Sometimes the honest answer is "this role is not the right fit for what you want
to build." Saying that honestly — with support for the transition, with genuine
goodwill — builds more trust than pretending the fit is there when it is not.

The organisation that helps someone leave gracefully when leaving is right for them
builds a reputation that attracts the people who should stay.

### Sharing the outcome honestly

If the organisation does well because employees worked hard, and employees see none
of that outcome, the relationship is extractive regardless of how warm the culture
feels. This is the founder salary problem: shared sacrifice requires genuinely shared
sacrifice — including when things go well.

The specific mechanism matters less than the principle. Profit sharing, meaningful
equity, bonus structures that reach below the senior layer — these are implementations
of a prior commitment: when we do well together, we do well together.

### The discretionary investment grows with seniority — or it does not

At junior levels, the cost of a flat relationship is limited. The junior employee
does the defined tasks adequately, and the organisation gets what it paid for.

At senior levels, the cost of a flat relationship is enormous — because the senior
contribution is almost entirely discretionary. The senior employee who is going
through the motions is present, productive by measurable standards, and absent in
every way that matters. The institutional memory they hold but do not share. The
junior employees they do not develop. The problems they notice but do not raise.
The culture they model by demonstrating that caring is performative and investment
is for suckers.

This is why the investment in genuine relationships is not altruism. It is the
mechanism by which seniority becomes increasingly valuable to the organisation
rather than increasingly coasting on accumulated status.

## What Genuine Care Is Mistaken For

### Perks

Perks are the corporate version of the contextual integrity violation. They produce
the appearance of care without the substance. A ping pong table is not the same
as a manager who knows what you are working toward. Free lunches are not the same
as autonomy over meaningful decisions. Unlimited PTO that the culture makes it
impossible to take is not the same as genuine respect for the employee's life
outside work.

People detect the difference. The relationship turns flat for exactly this reason:
the form of care arrived, the substance did not.

### The vocabulary of care without the practice

The leader who uses all the right phrases about psychological safety, growth mindset,
and employee wellbeing — but makes every important decision without consultation,
listens in meetings and ignores what they heard, disciplines people who raise concerns —
is performing care, not practicing it.

This is detectable over time and often produces a worse outcome than honest coldness.
The employee who was deceived about the relationship is more resentful than the
employee who always knew the terms were transactional.

### Retention without engagement

A company can retain people for years without engaging them. The switching costs
(vesting, benefits, social relationships, inertia) are real and effective. But
retention without engagement produces the actively disengaged employee — the one
Gallup identifies as actively working against the organisation. These are not lazy
people. They are people who had real expectations of a meaningful relationship
and were disappointed enough that the disappointment calcified into resentment.

## The Tarbiyat Dimension — Why It Cannot Be Faked at Scale

The organisation described above is not a culture deck or a benefits package.
It is a character question.

A leader who genuinely cares about the people working for them — who finds it
genuinely interesting to understand what someone needs, who feels actual discomfort
when someone is being treated unfairly, who can hear "I think you're wrong about this"
without it feeling like a threat — creates conditions where people want to show up.

That character cannot be replicated through training or policy. People are
extraordinarily good at detecting the difference between a leader who cares and
a leader who has learned the vocabulary of caring. The detection happens at the
level of what the leader does when no one is watching, when caring is costly,
when the honest thing conflicts with the convenient thing.

The companies that have done this longest share one feature: the care is not
a strategy. It is a prior commitment that shapes the strategy. The difference
between "we care about employees because it produces better business outcomes"
and "we care about employees because they are people and that is what caring means"
is detectable over time. The first position erodes when the business outcome
argument weakens. The second does not.

## The Gallup Data

Gallup has measured employee engagement globally for decades. The finding that
never changes:

- ~20-30% of employees are engaged — actively contributing discretionary effort
- ~50-60% are not engaged — present but going through the motions
- ~15-20% are actively disengaged — present and working against the organisation

The actively disengaged number is the most important. These are not bad people.
They are people who had expectations of a real relationship and were disappointed
enough that the disappointment turned to resentment. They are the result of the
half-way relationship — enough form to keep them present, not enough substance
to make presence matter.

The organisation that genuinely invests in the relationship does not reach 100%
engagement. It reaches the right 30-40% — the people who fit the culture, trust
its honesty, and choose to invest because the investment is real. That is enough.
A smaller group of people who genuinely show up is more valuable than a larger
group going through the motions, regardless of what the headcount numbers look like.

## The Practical Build Sequence

The question is not "what benefits package will retain employees?" It is "what kind
of workplace am I actually trying to build, and what does that require of me as the
person leading it?"

Answering that question honestly — including the parts where what the employee needs
conflicts with what is convenient, including the parts where genuine care requires
saying uncomfortable things — is the work. Everything else follows from it.

Applied to building:

**Before hiring:** be explicit about what you are building and what you are not.
The right people for a genuine culture are not necessarily the easiest people to
find or the cheapest to hire. They are the people for whom the substance of the
relationship — the honesty, the autonomy, the shared outcome — is what they actually
want, not a nice-to-have alongside a higher salary elsewhere.

**As seniority grows:** explicitly design for the discretionary investment. What
does the senior employee's contribution look like beyond their defined role? Are
they mentoring? Raising problems? Building culture? Carrying institutional knowledge
into the next generation? These contributions need to be seen, named, and valued —
not assumed to happen automatically because the employee is good at their job.

**When things go wrong:** the moment of genuine care is not when everything is
fine. It is when the organisation's interest and the employee's interest conflict.
How that moment is handled — whether the employee is seen as a person whose
interests matter in that conflict, or as a resource to be managed — is the data
that either builds or destroys the relationship for the long term.

---

## Key Insight

> The discretionary investment that makes seniority valuable cannot be purchased.
> It cannot be required. It cannot be produced by perks or performance review systems.
>
> It emerges from a specific kind of relationship — one where the employee believes,
> from accumulated experience over time, that the organisation is genuinely oriented
> toward their flourishing.
>
> Building that relationship is not an HR strategy.
> It is a character question — about what the leader actually cares about
> when caring is costly, when honesty is uncomfortable, and when the
> employee's interest and the organisation's convenience conflict.
>
> The organisations that have sustained this for decades share one feature:
> the care was never a strategy. It was a prior commitment.
> Everything else was built on top of it.
