# Hiring — Craft vs Credential, and the Honest Mutual Expectation

*Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis.*

---

## The Tension Nobody Names Honestly

There is a tension between what new hires expect and what companies actually need —
and neither side states it honestly, which means it never gets resolved.

**What the company actually needs:**

A new hire who can be productive quickly. Not because companies are heartless, but
because the economics of employment require it. Every week someone is not contributing
is a cost — salary, management time, opportunity cost of the work not getting done.
The company is not a training programme. It is an organisation trying to accomplish
something, that needs capable people to help accomplish it.

**What universities have been selling:**

The degree as a signal of readiness. "You have the credential, you are prepared."
This was always partially a fiction. It worked when the credential was selective
enough to actually filter for capability. When 445 people graduate from the same
CS program that produced 30-40 a generation ago, the credential stops being informative.
The company cannot rely on it as a proxy for readiness.

**What graduates often expect:**

That the credential confers respect, that the company will invest in developing their
potential, that seniority is available as a near-term aspiration rather than something
earned through demonstrated contribution over time. That the relationship will
resemble the education relationship — where the institution is responsible for
developing you.

The company and the graduate are in different relationships. The company is not
paying for potential. It is paying for contribution.

## The Credential Inflation Structural Diagnosis

Universities expanded to capture tuition revenue. Students enrolled to capture the
credential. Companies hired the credential. Everyone optimised for their own interest
in the transaction. Nobody was accountable for whether the credential actually meant
anything.

The result: graduates with the signal without the substance, who expect the respect
the signal used to confer but that the substance does not yet justify.

Companies responded by raising formal requirements — master's degrees for roles that
used to require bachelor's, bachelor's degrees for roles that used to require nothing.
The requirements went up without tracking capability better. This created a filter
that is simultaneously too expensive for the candidate and too weak for the company.

**The worst of both worlds:**
- Graduates spent four years and significant money on a credential that does not prove
  readiness
- Companies required that credential while knowing it does not prove readiness
- Companies then wonder why new hires need extensive support after passing the filter

The education system was monetised before it was accountable. Universities competed
for tuition revenue, expanded enrollment, reduced the selectivity that made the
credential meaningful. When credential inflation drove requirements higher, universities
expanded graduate programmes. The student carries the financial cost. The company
carries the productivity cost. The university captured the revenue and faced no
accountability for the outcome.

This is the same structure as every captured system we have discussed: the people
who built the system captured the benefit; the people who used it are carrying the cost.

## The Honest Mutual Expectation

### What the company owes the new hire

- Fair pay for the work being done
- Honest, specific feedback — including when performance is not meeting expectations
- Genuine opportunity to demonstrate capability and advance
- Domain knowledge about how the specific business works — this is legitimately the
  company's job to provide
- A manager who has enough time and interest to tell the employee what success
  looks like in this specific role

### What the new hire owes the company

- Functional craft capability before arriving. Not mastery. Not perfection.
  Functional capability: can write code that runs, can analyse data that means
  something, can communicate in writing that a reader who did not write it can
  follow. The technical foundation that the role requires — this is the employee's
  job to bring.
- Willingness to learn the domain quickly, without expecting the company to treat
  domain learning as the primary contribution
- The intellectual honesty to know the difference between genuine uncertainty
  (I have not encountered this problem before, I need guidance) and false uncertainty
  (I know roughly what to do but I am waiting to be told so I do not have to take
  responsibility for the decision)
- Contribution at the expected level within a reasonable timeframe — not perfection,
  but demonstration that the hire was sound

### The line between the two

Domain knowledge — how this business works, what these customers need, how these
systems are structured — is genuinely the company's responsibility to provide.
Every business has specific context that cannot be known before arrival.

Technical craft — the ability to actually do the thing the role requires at a
functional level — is the employee's responsibility to bring. It cannot be taught
at job speed. It requires the kind of sustained practice that only happens over
months and years, usually before employment.

When this line is violated in either direction, the relationship fails:
- Company refuses to teach domain knowledge → employee cannot contribute intelligently
- Employee lacks functional craft → company is subsidising basic training it cannot
  afford at the required pace

## The 3.1 GPA Problem — What to Actually Look For

The graduate who will be productive quickly is often not the one with the highest
GPA or the most prestigious credential.

They are often the one who:

- Built things before being told to — personal projects, open source contributions,
  freelance work, competition submissions — things that exist and can be evaluated
- Worked while studying — because real constraint forces real problem-solving in a
  way that coursework does not
- Has a track record of figuring things out rather than waiting to be taught
- Can explain what they built, why they made the choices they did, and what they
  would do differently — because they actually understand it rather than having
  executed instructions

This is the demonstrated work filter. Not the credential filter.

**Why the credential filter fails:**

The credential filter asks: where did you go, what grade did you get? These answer
the wrong question. The right question is: can you do the work?

**Why the demonstrated work filter works:**

You cannot fake a working project. A GitHub repository with commits over time, a
deployed application, a competition result, a freelance client who got what they
paid for — these are direct evidence of the capability the role requires.

The person who built something real under real constraint is making a different
claim than the person who passed coursework. The coursework claim is: I can perform
in an environment designed for my success. The demonstrated work claim is: I can
figure things out when the outcome is not predetermined.

## The Epidemic — Where It Came From

The credential inflation that produced the current situation is not random. It has
a specific cause: the disconnection between who pays for credentials and who validates
them.

Students pay universities for credentials. Universities compete for students by
lowering barriers to success (grade inflation, expanding programs, reducing academic
rigour). Companies hire based on credentials. Nobody in this chain is accountable
for whether the credential tracks the capability the company actually needs.

The student who graduates with a 3.8 GPA from an expanded program where a 3.8 is
the median is carrying a signal that has been inflated to near-meaninglessness.
They are not responsible for the inflation. They were responding rationally to the
incentives they faced. But the company that hires them based on the GPA and credential
without evaluating actual capability is making a mistake — and the graduate who
expects the company to respond to the credential as if it still meant what it used
to mean is carrying an expectation that was sold to them but does not currently hold.

**The structural fix — not available to individual companies:**

The accountability gap (universities not accountable for whether credentials track
capability) requires systemic response — things like income share agreements,
employer-university partnerships that include outcome tracking, coding bootcamp
models that charge on employment rather than enrollment. These are partial and
imperfect but they move the incentive in the right direction.

**The practical fix — available to individual companies:**

Stop using the credential as the primary filter. Use demonstrated work. Use a
skills assessment that is relevant to the actual role. Use trial projects. Use
probationary periods with honest evaluation at the end. Make the expectation explicit
before the hire is made: here is what functional capability looks like in this role,
here is how we will evaluate whether you have it in the first 90 days, here is
what happens if you do and here is what happens if you do not.

Honesty before the hire prevents the disappointment that produces resentment after.

## What This Means for Building the Company

If you want people who can contribute quickly, who will grow into senior contributors
who invest in the organisation because the organisation invests in them, the hire
has to be honest from the start.

**The honest pitch to potential hires:**

"We will teach you how this business works, what our customers need, and how our
systems are structured. We will give you genuine autonomy as you demonstrate
capability, honest feedback always, and a share of the outcome when we succeed
together. We will not teach you your craft. That is what you bring. If you bring it,
this place will be worth your best work. If you do not bring it, we will know quickly
and we will be honest about it."

**The demonstrated work filter applied:**

Not "where did you go to school?" but "show me what you built."
Not "what is your GPA?" but "walk me through a decision you made under real constraint."
Not "what do you know?" but "here is a real problem from our actual work — what do you do?"

The person who can answer the third question well — who approaches an unfamiliar
real problem with genuine curiosity and structured thinking — is almost always the
person who built things before being told to, who worked under real constraint, who
developed the craft because they cared about it rather than because it was required.

That person exists in every cohort. They are not the majority of graduates.
They are findable — if you are looking for what they demonstrate rather than what
they credential.

---

## Key Insight

> The company is not a training programme. The university is not a talent pipeline.
> The credential is not proof of craft.
>
> The graduate who will contribute quickly and grow into a senior who invests in
> the organisation is the one who already took ownership of their own development —
> who did not wait to be taught, who built things under real constraint, who can
> demonstrate capability rather than credential.
>
> The hiring process that finds them is the demonstrated work filter, not the
> credential filter. The relationship that keeps them is the genuine one —
> where the company provides domain knowledge, honest feedback, real autonomy,
> and shared outcome, and the employee provides craft, contribution, and the
> intellectual honesty to know the difference between genuine uncertainty
> and the kind that is waiting to be told.
>
> State the terms honestly before the hire.
> Evaluate honestly during it.
> The relationship that forms on honest terms is the one that produces
> the discretionary investment that makes seniority worth having.
