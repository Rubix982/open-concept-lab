# Neoliberalism as Operating System

*Personal study notes — original analysis and synthesis. Not a reproduction of course material.*

---

## The Core Observation

Neoliberalism is not one issue among many. It is the operating system that most of
the problems in AI governance, technology regulation, and political economy are running
on. Once you see it, it is difficult to explain almost anything in contemporary politics,
economics, or technology without referencing it — because it shapes the assumptions,
the vocabulary, the incentive structures, and the range of acceptable solutions in
almost every domain simultaneously.

Its power comes from presenting itself as the absence of ideology. Markets are natural.
Efficiency is neutral. Growth is just what economies do. The normative choices embedded
in those framings are invisible because they are the water everyone is swimming in.

---

## Where Neoliberalism Appears as the Hidden Variable

Almost nothing in AI governance is fully explicable without it:

**AGI and capital concentration** — the race narrative only makes sense in a context
where winner-takes-all market dynamics are assumed to be natural and inevitable. A
different economic framework would produce a different approach to transformative
technology entirely.

**Regulatory capture** — only works at scale in a system where money flows freely
into political processes and where the revolving door between industry and government
is treated as normal career progression rather than corruption.

**"Government overreach" as an argument** — is a neoliberal product, manufactured and
distributed by neoliberal institutions to protect neoliberal interests. It barely exists
as a serious political position outside contexts where neoliberalism is dominant.

**Efficiency as invisible normative default** — the framework of measuring economic
health by GDP growth and productivity replaced earlier frameworks that included full
employment, wage growth, and social stability as explicit goals of policy.

**The think tank industry** — built deliberately in the 1970s and 1980s to produce
the intellectual infrastructure for the political project. The Powell Memo (1971) is
the founding document — a direct call for corporate America to fund institutions that
would shift the political and intellectual climate.

**Data extraction as a business model** — only makes sense in a system with no
meaningful constraint on what can be commodified, where cognitive labour producing
data does not need to be compensated, and where the state lacks the will to enforce
boundaries.

**Time poverty** — the direct result of a labour market organised around extracting
maximum productive hours at minimum cost, with no countervailing power to defend
workers' time.

**Sycophancy amplified** — when career advancement depends on pleasing those with
money and power, and when that relationship is the primary organising principle of
professional life, the incentive to tell uncomfortable truths is structurally suppressed
at every level.

**The KSA case** — a specific interaction between resource-rent capitalism and
neoliberal consulting culture. Western advisors brought a neoliberal framework —
markets, efficiency, liberalisation — and applied it to a context it wasn't designed
for, without accountability for the outcome.

## The Winner-Takes-All Endgame — What Do You Actually Win?

The winner-takes-all logic has never been properly interrogated at its conclusion.
What does the winner actually get?

Control of a system where:
- The people who would buy your products cannot afford them
- The workers who would build your products are too precarious to be reliable
- The infrastructure your business depends on is crumbling because no one invested in it
- The political system is so captured it cannot solve the problems eating your market
- The social fabric is torn enough that security, trust, and stability — the actual
  foundations of commerce — are deteriorating

**This is not hypothetical.** The wealthiest individuals in human history are spending
their fortunes trying to fix political dysfunction, infrastructure decay, and
civilisational problems that are the direct downstream consequences of the system
that made them wealthy. The winners are beginning to notice that what they won is
a deteriorating asset.

**The feudalism analogy.** Medieval lords won everything — land, serfs, castles, armies.
And then they lived in a world of plague, starvation, constant warfare, no sanitation,
no reliable food supply. They won the game and the game was terrible. The peasants
were miserable. The lords were also miserable — they just had more decorative misery.

**The Ford insight the current generation forgot.** Henry Ford paid his workers enough
to buy Fords — not out of generosity, but because he understood that a consumer economy
requires consumers. You cannot extract all the value from labour and expect labour to
purchase what you produce. The math does not close. The current AI deployment trajectory
is heading toward this contradiction at civilisational scale — systems that eliminate
the economic participation of large portions of the population, in an economy that
requires their participation to function.

## What the Winner Actually Inherits

In a fully captured, fully unequal, fully precarious society, the winner gets:

- A security apparatus that costs more every year because inequality produces crime,
  instability, and resentment
- A political system so dysfunctional it cannot address collective problems —
  including the ones threatening the winner's own interests
- A population too stressed, too tired, and too precarious to produce the creativity,
  innovation, and social trust that drive long-term prosperity
- Children who inherit wealth but not the conditions that made wealth generative
- A physical environment degraded by the externalities nobody was made to pay for

**The stability point is underrated.** The most valuable thing for long-term economic
activity is stability — predictable rules, reliable institutions, social trust, physical
security, a population healthy enough to work and wealthy enough to consume. All of
these are public goods. All are produced by exactly the kind of government investment
and social infrastructure that winner-takes-all ideology systematically defunds.

The winners are sawing off the branch they are sitting on and calling it efficiency.

**Private infrastructure has a ceiling.** The wealthy build private security, private
healthcare, private education, private water supplies. But private infrastructure is
always inferior to functional public infrastructure at scale. You cannot privately
build a functioning city. You cannot privately maintain the legal and institutional
predictability that commerce requires. At some point the deterioration of the public
system degrades the private alternatives too.

The winner takes all. Then has to guard it. Then has to maintain it. Then watches it
decay because the system that produced it is gone.

## Neoliberalism's Documented History

This is not a conspiracy theory. It is a framework with named architects, traceable
funding, and measurable consequences.

**The intellectual architects:**
- Friedrich Hayek — *The Road to Serfdom* (1944) — markets as the only legitimate
  information processing system; government intervention as the path to tyranny
- Milton Friedman — *Capitalism and Freedom* (1962) — monetarism, deregulation,
  shareholder primacy; the corporation's only obligation is profit
- The Chicago School — the academic home of the framework, substantially funded by
  corporate interests from the 1950s onward

**The political deployment:**
- Powell Memo (1971) — Lewis Powell's memo to the US Chamber of Commerce calling
  for systematic corporate funding of think tanks, academic chairs, and media to
  shift the intellectual climate
- Thatcher (1979) and Reagan (1980) — political implementation; "there is no
  alternative," deregulation, union-busting, financialisation
- Washington Consensus (1989) — the export of the framework globally through the
  IMF and World Bank as conditions on development loans

**The measurable consequences (1980-present):**
- Productivity grew substantially
- Wages for the bottom 60% stagnated or declined in real terms
- Wealth concentration accelerated in every adopting country
- Social mobility declined
- Public infrastructure deteriorated
- The 2008 financial crisis — the most efficient financial markets in history
  requiring massive state intervention to prevent total system failure

The efficiency was real. The distribution was not what the framework promised.
The costs were borne by people who had no say in the choice.

## Why AI Is Neoliberalism's Latest Chapter

AI is not arriving into a neutral context. It is arriving into a context already
shaped by 40-50 years of the framework described above. It will be developed,
deployed, and governed within that context unless the context is explicitly named
and challenged.

The specific risks:
- AI productivity gains will follow the same distribution pattern as previous
  automation waves — concentrated gains, diffuse costs — unless the framework is changed
- AI governance will be shaped by the same captured institutions — unless structural
  change in governance is demanded
- The "move fast" urgency will serve the same interests — unless the pace question
  is treated as a values question, not a technical one
- "AI ethics" will serve as the latest form of managed dissent — unless rights, law,
  liability, and representation replace it as the operative vocabulary

The people most affected by AI — workers facing displacement, communities under
surveillance, populations whose data trains systems sold back to them — are the same
people most affected by neoliberalism. That is not coincidence. It is the same
system, in a new phase.

## What Seeing It Changes

Naming neoliberalism as the operating system does not solve the problems it produces.
But it changes what solutions are visible.

If you don't see it, the available solutions are: better ethics frameworks, more
responsible corporate behaviour, improved public communication about AI risks.
All of these operate within the system and cannot challenge its foundations.

If you see it, the available solutions expand to include: redistribution of power
through law and enforcement, antitrust action on compute concentration, mandatory
representation of affected communities, liability frameworks that align incentives,
international coordination that doesn't defer to the interests of the most powerful actors.

The difference is not academic. It is the difference between asking the system to
be nicer and asking the system to change.

## The China Threat Narrative — Manufactured Urgency

The "China is winning the AI race" framing serves specific domestic interests:

- Defence contractors who need a threat to justify budgets
- Tech companies who need urgency to justify deregulation and speed
- Politicians who need a foreign enemy to unify a fragmented electorate
- Investors who need FOMO to drive capital into AI at speed

China is a real competitor in specific domains. But the framing of an existential
race that justifies abandoning environmental, social, and regulatory considerations
is a political product, not an objective assessment.

**The BYD/Tesla contradiction.**

BYD makes better electric vehicles at lower prices. By every market logic that
American capitalism claims to believe in — competition, efficiency, consumer choice
— BYD should operate freely in the US market. It doesn't, because the US government
blocked it through tariffs and import restrictions designed to protect a domestic
incumbent.

The same political system that argues regulation distorts markets and government
shouldn't interfere in competition used government intervention to protect Tesla
from competition. The "free market" principle applies when it benefits the right
people and disappears when it doesn't.

Who won? China built a domestic EV industry that now dominates global export markets
— through exactly the state-directed industrial policy that American political culture
claims to oppose. The result: cheaper, better electric vehicles for most of the world,
except America, which protected its market and got more expensive, less competitive cars.

- If the goal was climate — more EVs globally — China won
- If the goal was consumer benefit — BYD won
- If the goal was American market dominance — America intervened to protect it
- The only goal Tesla's protection served was Tesla's shareholders

**China will remain for China.**

Chinese technological advancement does not automatically threaten America the way the
narrative implies. China building better AI, better EVs, better infrastructure —
primarily makes China better. The zero-sum framing — if China wins, America loses —
is a geopolitical choice made by people who benefit from the conflict framing, not an
objective description of how technological progress works.

Japan built the world's best manufacturing systems in the 1970s and 80s. America
panicked. The result was not American collapse — it was pressure on American industry
to improve, and a generation of better products globally. Competition between capable
actors benefits everyone who gets access to the outputs.

**The race narrative serves the companies, not the citizens.**

The US is not racing China for the benefit of its citizens. It is racing China for
the benefit of companies whose shareholders profit from winning. The citizens of
both countries would benefit more from cooperation on the things that actually threaten
them — climate, pandemic preparedness, water scarcity, ecological collapse — than
from competition on who can build the most datacenters fastest.

But cooperation doesn't sell defence budgets. It doesn't justify deregulation.
So the race narrative persists — and the environmental, social, and democratic
considerations get discarded in its name.

## "Difficult But Not Impossible" — The Regulatory Momentum Problem

The course identifies a regulatory gap: "it is currently very difficult to assess
the energy or environmental price of building a datacenter." Transparency reports
are suggested as a starting point.

**"Difficult" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.**

We measure electricity consumption for every household. We meter water for every
building. We track carbon emissions from factories under existing law. The
instrumentation exists. The measurement methodology exists. The technology exists.

What doesn't exist is the political will to require it from an industry that has
successfully argued it shouldn't have to.

"Difficult" in regulatory language almost always means one of three things:

1. **Technically difficult** — genuinely hard, requires new methodology.
   Rarely the actual case — usually the measurement exists, just hasn't been mandated.
2. **Politically difficult** — the entities that would need to report have enough
   influence to prevent the requirement. Almost always the real meaning.
3. **Expensive for the regulated entity** — disclosure costs money, creates liability,
   reveals information they'd rather keep private. Companies call this "difficult."

In the datacenter case: overwhelmingly categories 2 and 3. The technical difficulty
is real but minor — a few years of standardisation work, the kind of thing industry
does routinely when it serves their interests.

**The chokepoint chain — where momentum dies:**

```
Measurement standard agreed
        ↓  ← lobby to make it complex, voluntary, self-reported
Disclosure mandated
        ↓  ← argue proprietary, burdensome, premature
Data becomes public
        ↓  ← claim competitive sensitivity, fight in court
Cost of externalities becomes visible
        ↓  ← fund counter-narratives, capture the regulatory body
Political pressure for levies builds
        ↓  ← lobby directly, fund opposing campaigns, threaten relocation
Levy legislation passes
        ↓
Infrastructure fund operates
```

The solution exists at the end of the chain. The industry has blocked every link.
Not through a single dramatic intervention — through sustained, well-funded, patient
obstruction at every point where momentum could build.

**What actually builds momentum:**

- **Crisis** — a water table collapses visibly, a grid fails, a community's health
  outcomes become undeniable and publicly attributed. Korangi-scale visibility,
  applied to AI infrastructure.
- **Organised pressure** — communities affected by industrial pollution building
  coalitions with climate advocates, water rights groups, agricultural interests.
  The Venn diagram is large. It needs to be organised.
- **International coordination** — the EU moves first, creates a standard, and trade
  pressure forces others to follow. The EU AI Act and Corporate Sustainability
  Reporting Directive are early steps.
- **Legal liability** — a court case establishes that a company is liable for
  environmental damage from undisclosed datacenter operations. Immediate financial
  incentive for transparency follows.

The measurement framework is the first domino. Once measurement exists, the cost
becomes visible. Once visible, it becomes political. Once political, the momentum shifts.

The solution is not missing. The political will to push the first domino is missing.
That is a different problem — and a solvable one.

## Key Insight

> The winner-takes-all endgame is a broken society with broken rules,
> guarded by people who cannot fix it because fixing it would require
> distributing what they accumulated.
>
> Neoliberalism promised that the tide would lift all boats.
> The tide lifted the yachts. The dinghies are underwater.
> And the people on the yachts are wondering why nobody is buying their products.
>
> AI deployed into this context without accountability will not fix it.
> It will accelerate it — until the acceleration becomes the crisis
> that forces the reckoning that should have happened 40 years ago.
